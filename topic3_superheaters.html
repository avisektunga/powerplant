3. Steam Power-Ups: Superheaters, Reheaters & Temp Controllers!

Superheater: Giving Steam an Extra Energy Kick!

Imagine regular steam from a boiling kettle – that's "saturated steam." It's hot, but it still contains tiny water droplets or is just about to condense. A **superheater** is like a special afterburner section in the boiler that takes this saturated steam and heats it even *more*, without increasing its pressure. This "dried and energized" steam is called **superheated steam**.

Self-Question: Why bother superheating? Isn't saturated steam good enough?

Answer: Good question! Superheated steam is like premium fuel for turbines. It packs more energy, makes the power plant more efficient, AND it's drier, which is gentler on the turbine blades, preventing them from getting eroded by water droplets.

  • Main Jobs of a Superheater:
    • Boost Power Plant Efficiency: Hotter steam = more efficient energy conversion in the turbine (think higher average temperature in the Rankine cycle).
    • Protect Turbine Blades: Reduces moisture in the steam as it expands through the turbine, preventing those tiny water bullets from damaging the blades.
    • Reduce Steam Consumption: Turbines need less superheated steam to produce the same amount of power compared to saturated steam.
  • Types of Superheaters (How they grab heat):
    • Radiant Superheater: Sits right in the furnace, soaking up heat like sunbathing (radiation from flames). Its steam temperature tends to *drop* a bit if the boiler load increases.
    • Convective Superheater: Placed in the path of hot flue gases after the furnace, getting heated by the "wind" of these gases (convection). Its steam temperature tends to *rise* with increasing boiler load.
    • Combined Superheater: Uses both radiant and convective sections to get a nice, stable steam temperature across different loads. Clever!

Reheater: The Steam "Pit Stop" for an Energy Refill!

In big power plants, steam works really hard expanding through the high-pressure (HP) part of the turbine. By the time it's done there, it's lost a lot of energy and temperature. Instead of sending it straight to the low-pressure (LP) turbine stages (where it might get too wet and tired), we give it a "pit stop"!

Analogy: A Multi-Stage Rocket. The first stage gets it high, then drops off. The second stage (reheated steam) then fires up with fresh energy to go even further!
  • The Reheat Cycle Process:
    1. Hot, high-pressure superheated steam does its job in the HP turbine.
    2. This "tired" steam (now at lower pressure) is piped *back* to the boiler.
    3. Inside the boiler, it passes through a **Reheater** (another set of tubes heated by flue gases, just like a superheater).
    4. The steam gets its temperature boosted again (reheated!), usually to near its original superheat temperature.
    5. This re-energized steam then zips off to the intermediate-pressure (IP) and/or LP turbine sections to do more work.
  • Why Reheat? The Benefits:
    • More Efficiency, More Power!: Increases the overall thermal efficiency of the power plant.
    • Happier Turbines: Keeps the steam drier in the LP turbine stages, reducing blade erosion and improving turbine efficiency.
    • More Bang for Your Buck: Gets more work out of each kilogram of steam.

T-S Diagram: See that extra hump? That's the reheat giving steam more energy!

Attemperator (Desuperheater): The Steam Thermostat!

Sometimes, the superheated (or reheated) steam can get *too* hot, especially if the boiler load changes or the fire is a bit too feisty. An **Attemperator** (or Desuperheater) acts like a thermostat to cool it down to the perfect temperature.

Self-Question: Why would *too* hot steam be a problem?

Answer: Super-hot steam can damage expensive equipment like the final superheater tubes themselves, or worse, the very precisely engineered turbine blades and casings. They are designed for specific temperature limits!

  • Main Purpose: To accurately control and reduce the temperature of superheated or reheated steam.
  • How it Works (The Cool-Down Spray): The most common type, a **Spray Attemperator**, injects a fine spray of high-purity, cooler water (usually feedwater) directly into the hot steam path. This water evaporates instantly, absorbing some of the steam's heat and cooling it down.
    Analogy: Like a quick, fine mist spray on a hot day to cool you down.
  • Other Types:
    • Surface Type: Steam passes over tubes that have cooler water flowing inside them. No direct mixing. Slower, but sometimes preferred if spray water quality is a concern.
  • Where are they?: Often found between superheater stages or just before the final superheater outlet, and also used to control reheat steam temperature.

Previous Year Questions & Numerical Problems:

2017 Q8b: Efficiency analysis with economizer, superheater.

An economizer and a superheater are key components in a boiler system that significantly contribute to its overall thermal efficiency.

Economizer: The Feedwater Warmer-Upper!

  • Function: It's a heat exchanger that sneakily steals waste heat from the flue gases (exhaust fumes) *before* they escape up the chimney. This stolen heat is used to preheat the incoming feedwater.
  • Efficiency Boost:
    • Less fuel needed: Since feedwater is already warmer, the boiler needs less fuel to turn it into steam.
    • Cooler chimney gases: Means less heat wasted to the atmosphere.
    • Rule of thumb: Every 6-7°C rise in feedwater temperature can boost boiler efficiency by about 1%!

Superheater: The Steam Energizer!

  • Function: Takes saturated steam (steam at its boiling point for a given pressure) and heats it further to a higher temperature, making it "superheated."
  • Efficiency Boost (for the whole power plant):
    • More punch: Superheated steam has more energy to give to the turbine.
    • Better cycle: Increases the average temperature at which heat is added in the power cycle, which thermodynamics tells us is good for efficiency.
    • Turbine protection: Keeps steam dry through most of the turbine, preventing erosion of blades by water droplets.

In short: Economizers save fuel by recycling waste heat, and superheaters make the steam more potent for power generation, both leading to better overall efficiency.

2019 Q4: Function of attemperator.

The attemperator (or desuperheater) is the boiler's **precision temperature controller for superheated steam.** Its main job is to cool down superheated steam if it gets too hot, ensuring it's at the *exact* right temperature before it goes to the turbine or other processes.

Why is this so important?

  1. Protecting Pricey Equipment:
    • Turbines are like high-performance engines; they need steam at just the right temperature. Too hot, and you risk damage from thermal stress or even melting parts!
    • Even the superheater tubes themselves can get damaged if the steam inside isn't carrying away enough heat (which happens if its temperature gets out of control).
  2. Steady Performance: Boiler conditions can change (load, fuel quality). The attemperator helps keep the final steam temperature stable for consistent turbine performance.
  3. Reheat Control: In plants with reheaters, attemperators also fine-tune the temperature of the reheated steam.

How it works (usually): A fine spray of very pure, cooler water is injected into the hot steam. The water evaporates instantly, absorbing heat and cooling the steam down to the target temperature. It's a very precise balancing act!

2019 Q11b: Reheater in boiler.

A reheater in a boiler system is like a "second chance" for steam to gain more energy after it has already done some work.

The "Reheat" Journey:

  1. Fresh, superheated steam from the boiler rushes into the High-Pressure (HP) section of the turbine and expands, spinning the turbine and losing some of its pressure and temperature.
  2. Instead of letting this now "partially tired" steam continue expanding until it gets too cool and wet, it's piped *back* to the boiler.
  3. Inside the boiler, this steam flows through a special set of tubes called the **Reheater**. These tubes are heated by the hot furnace gases, just like the superheater tubes.
  4. The steam gets its temperature significantly boosted again (reheated), usually close to its original superheat temperature, although its pressure is now lower than when it first entered the turbine.
  5. This re-energized, hot steam then goes to the Intermediate-Pressure (IP) and/or Low-Pressure (LP) sections of the turbine to do more work before finally going to the condenser.

Why bother with this "pit stop"?

  • More Power & Efficiency: It increases the overall thermal efficiency of the power plant cycle. You get more work out of the same amount of initial fuel.
  • Keeps Turbine Blades Happy: By reheating, the steam stays drier (less moisture) as it expands through the later stages of the turbine. Wet steam can erode turbine blades like tiny sandblasters, so drier steam means longer turbine life and better performance.

Reheaters are common in large, modern power plants where maximizing efficiency is crucial.

Numerical: Marine Boiler Efficiency Calculation

Problem: Calculate the efficiency of a marine boiler operating under the following conditions: feed water at 50 bar and 120 °C is supplied to the boiler at the rate of 30 kg/s. Of this, 25 kg/s leaves the superheater outlet at 40 bar and 450 °C and the remainder leaves the steam drum as dry saturated steam at 42 bar for auxiliary purposes. The fuel used is oil having CV of 40,414 MJ/m³ and is supplied at the rate of 7.2 m³/h.

Given Data:

  • Total feedwater supply rate ($\dot{m}_{fw}$) = 30 kg/s
  • Feedwater condition: $P_{fw}$ = 50 bar, $T_{fw}$ = 120 °C
  • Superheated steam: $\dot{m}_{sh}$ = 25 kg/s, $P_{sh}$ = 40 bar, $T_{sh}$ = 450 °C
  • Auxiliary steam (dry saturated): $\dot{m}_{aux}$ = 30 - 25 = 5 kg/s, $P_{aux}$ = 42 bar
  • Fuel oil CV = 40,414 MJ/m³ = 40,414,000 kJ/m³
  • Fuel supply rate ($\dot{V}_f$) = 7.2 m³/h

Step 1: Determine Enthalpies from Steam Tables

We need the following enthalpies (h):

  1. $h_{fw}$ (Enthalpy of feedwater): At 50 bar, 120 °C. Since $T_{sat}$ at 50 bar is approx 264 °C, the feedwater is subcooled liquid. $h_{fw} \approx h_f \text{ at } 120 °C \approx 503.7 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  2. $h_{sh}$ (Enthalpy of superheated steam): At 40 bar, 450 °C. From superheated steam tables, $h_{sh} \approx 3330.3 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  3. $h_{aux}$ (Enthalpy of dry saturated steam for auxiliary): At 42 bar. From saturated steam tables, $h_g \text{ at } 42 \text{ bar} (h_{aux}) \approx 2800.3 \text{ kJ/kg}$.

(Note: Use specific steam tables for accurate values.)

Step 2: Calculate Heat Output of the Boiler ($Q_{out}$)

Heat absorbed for superheated steam = $\dot{m}_{sh} \times (h_{sh} - h_{fw})$

= 25 kg/s $\times$ (3330.3 - 503.7) kJ/kg = 25 $\times$ 2826.6 = 70665 kW

Heat absorbed for auxiliary steam = $\dot{m}_{aux} \times (h_{aux} - h_{fw})$

= 5 kg/s $\times$ (2800.3 - 503.7) kJ/kg = 5 $\times$ 2296.6 = 11483 kW

Total Heat Output ($Q_{out}$) = 70665 kW + 11483 kW = 82148 kW

Step 3: Calculate Heat Input from Fuel ($Q_{in}$)

Fuel supply rate $\dot{V}_f = 7.2 \text{ m}^3/\text{h} = 7.2 / 3600 \text{ m}^3/\text{s} = 0.002 \text{ m}^3/\text{s}$.

Heat Input ($Q_{in}$) = $\dot{V}_f \times \text{CV} = 0.002 \text{ m}^3/\text{s} \times 40,414,000 \text{ kJ/m}^3 = 80828 \text{ kW}$.

Step 4: Calculate Boiler Efficiency ($\eta_{boiler}$)

$\eta_{boiler} = (Q_{out} / Q_{in}) \times 100\% = (82148 \text{ kW} / 80828 \text{ kW}) \times 100\% \approx 101.63\%$

Analysis of Result:

An efficiency > 100% is impossible. This indicates an inconsistency in the provided data (e.g., fuel consumption too low for steam output, or CV error). Always state this in an exam if such a result occurs, then provide the answer based on calculation.

Calculated Efficiency (with given data): $\eta_{boiler} \approx 101.63\%$. (Physically impossible, indicates data inconsistency).

Numerical: Reheat Cycle Analysis

Problem: In a reheat cycle, the initial steam pressure and the maximum temperature are 150 bar and 550 °C respectively. If the condenser pressure is 0.1 bar and dryness fraction is 0.95 at condenser inlet then determine (i) Reheat Pressure, (ii) The cycle efficiency, (iii) The steam rate. (Use Mollier diagram or steam tables).

Given Data:
  • State 1 (HP Turbine Inlet): $P_1 = 150 \text{ bar}$, $T_1 = 550^\circ\text{C}$
  • State 4 (LP Turbine Outlet/Condenser Inlet): $P_4 = 0.1 \text{ bar}$, $x_4 = 0.95$
  • State 3 (IP/LP Turbine Inlet after Reheat): $T_3 = 550^\circ\text{C}$ (Max temp, usually reheat to initial temp)

This problem requires detailed thermodynamic cycle analysis using steam tables or a Mollier chart. The solution involves finding enthalpies and entropies at various state points.

Methodology Outline:
  1. State 1 (HP Turbine Inlet): From steam tables for $P_1, T_1$, find $h_1$ and $s_1$.
    (Approx: $h_1 \approx 3448.6 \text{ kJ/kg}, s_1 \approx 6.5197 \text{ kJ/kgK}$)
  2. State 4 (Condenser Inlet): At $P_4=0.1 \text{ bar}, x_4=0.95$. Find $h_4$ and $s_4$.
    $h_f @0.1\text{bar} \approx 191.81 \text{ kJ/kg}$, $h_{fg} @0.1\text{bar} \approx 2392.1 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $h_4 = h_f + x_4 h_{fg} = 191.81 + 0.95 \times 2392.1 \approx 2464.3 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $s_f @0.1\text{bar} \approx 0.6492 \text{ kJ/kgK}$, $s_{fg} @0.1\text{bar} \approx 7.4995 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    $s_4 = s_f + x_4 s_{fg} = 0.6492 + 0.95 \times 7.4995 \approx 7.7737 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
  3. (i) Reheat Pressure ($P_{reheat} = P_2 = P_3$):
    Assume isentropic expansion in IP/LP turbine (State 3 to State 4), so $s_3 = s_4 \approx 7.7737 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    State 3 is $T_3 = 550^\circ\text{C}$ and $s_3 \approx 7.7737 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    From superheated steam tables (or Mollier chart), find $P_3$ (by interpolation if needed) corresponding to $T_3$ and $s_3$. This $P_3$ is the Reheat Pressure. Also find $h_3$.
    (This requires table lookup. Let's assume for example this yields $P_3 \approx 30 \text{ bar}$ and $h_3 \approx 3560 \text{ kJ/kg}$ - actual values will vary based on precise table lookup.)
  4. State 2 (HP Turbine Outlet/Reheater Inlet):
    Assume isentropic expansion in HP turbine (State 1 to State 2), so $s_2 = s_1 \approx 6.5197 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    At pressure $P_2 = P_3$ (reheat pressure found above) and $s_2$, find $h_2$ from steam tables.
    (e.g., if $P_2=30 \text{ bar}$ and $s_2=6.5197$, find $h_2 \approx 2990 \text{ kJ/kg}$).
  5. State 5 (Condenser Outlet/Pump Inlet): Saturated liquid at $P_5 = P_4 = 0.1 \text{ bar}$.
    $h_5 = h_{f @0.1\text{bar}} \approx 191.81 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $v_5 = v_{f @0.1\text{bar}} \approx 0.001010 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg}$.
  6. State 6 (Pump Outlet/Boiler Inlet):
    Pump work $W_p = v_5 (P_6 - P_5) = v_5 (P_1 - P_4)$ assuming one pump.
    $W_p = 0.001010 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg} \times (150 - 0.1) \times 100 \text{ kPa/bar} \approx 15.14 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $h_6 = h_5 + W_p = 191.81 + 15.14 \approx 206.95 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  7. (ii) Cycle Efficiency ($\eta_{cycle}$):
    Turbine Work $W_T = (h_1 - h_2) + (h_3 - h_4)$.
    Heat Supplied $Q_{in} = (h_1 - h_6) + (h_3 - h_2)$ (heat in boiler + heat in reheater).
    Net Work $W_{net} = W_T - W_p$.
    $\eta_{cycle} = W_{net} / Q_{in}$

    (Substitute example enthalpy values to get a numerical result).
    $W_T = (3448.6 - 2990) + (3560 - 2464.3) = 458.6 + 1095.7 = 1554.3 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $Q_{in} = (3448.6 - 206.95) + (3560 - 2990) = 3241.65 + 570 = 3811.65 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $W_{net} = 1554.3 - 15.14 = 1539.16 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $\eta_{cycle} = 1539.16 / 3811.65 \approx 0.4038 \text{ or } 40.38\%$.
  8. (iii) Steam Rate (SR):
    $SR = 3600 / W_{net} \text{ (kg/kWh)}$

    $SR = 3600 / 1539.16 \approx 2.339 \text{ kg/kWh}$.

(Using example interpolated values for P_reheat $\approx 30$ bar):
(i) Reheat Pressure $\approx 30 \text{ bar}$ (Requires precise chart/table lookup for accuracy).
(ii) Cycle Efficiency $\approx 40.38\%$.
(iii) Steam Rate $\approx 2.339 \text{ kg/kWh}$.

Important: The accuracy of these results heavily depends on precise steam table or Mollier chart usage. The values used for $P_3, h_3, h_2$ are illustrative based on typical trends. Students must perform the actual lookups.

MCQ Power-Up Quiz!

1. Water for attemperation (desuperheating) is typically taken from the:

  • a) Boiler drum, because it's hot.
  • b) Economizer outlet (after feedwater pump), because it's high-purity and high-pressure.
  • c) Condenser hotwell, because it's cool.
  • d) Deaerator, for oxygen removal.

Answer: b) Economizer outlet (after feedwater pump), because it's high-purity and high-pressure.

2. If you want to make your power plant more efficient and also protect your turbine blades from excessive moisture, you would primarily use a:

  • a) Bigger boiler drum
  • b) Superheater
  • c) Larger condenser
  • d) Forced draught fan

Answer: b) Superheater

3. In a reheat cycle, where does the "reheating" of steam happen?

  • a) Inside the high-pressure turbine.
  • b) In a special section of the condenser.
  • c) Back inside the boiler, in reheater tubes.
  • d) Just before entering the boiler feed pump.

Answer: c) Back inside the boiler, in reheater tubes.

4. A radiant superheater gets most of its heat from:

  • a) Hot flue gases passing over its tubes (convection).
  • b) Direct radiation from the flames and hot furnace walls.
  • c) The economizer.
  • d) The boiler drum.

Answer: b) Direct radiation from the flames and hot furnace walls.

5. What is the main risk if superheated steam temperature becomes TOO high for the turbine?

  • a) The steam will condense too quickly.
  • b) The boiler efficiency will drop sharply.
  • c) Thermal stress and potential damage to turbine components.
  • d) The condenser will not be able to create a vacuum.

Answer: c) Thermal stress and potential damage to turbine components.

6. The steam temperature characteristic of a convective superheater typically shows that as boiler load increases, the outlet steam temperature:

  • a) Decreases significantly
  • b) Remains perfectly constant
  • c) Tends to increase
  • d) Fluctuates randomly

Answer: c) Tends to increase (due to higher gas flow and temperature over tubes).

7. One of the primary thermodynamic benefits of a reheat cycle is:

  • a) It significantly increases the pressure of the steam entering the LP turbine.
  • b) It increases the average temperature at which heat is rejected from the cycle.
  • c) It increases the average temperature at which heat is supplied to the cycle.
  • d) It eliminates the need for a condenser.

Answer: c) It increases the average temperature at which heat is supplied to the cycle.
