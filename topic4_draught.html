What's Draught? Giving the Boiler Room to Breathe!

Imagine trying to light a barbecue without any airflow – it just smolders, right? A boiler is similar! **Draught (or Draft)** is the gentle "pull" or "push" (a small pressure difference) that makes air flow *into* the furnace for burning fuel and then pushes the smoky exhaust gases (flue gases) *out* through the chimney.

Without good draught, your boiler would be a smoky, inefficient mess! It's essential for:

  • Supplying enough fresh air (oxygen!) for the fire to burn brightly and completely.
  • Sweeping away the combustion products (smoke and ash) from the furnace.
  • Sending those flue gases high up into the atmosphere via the chimney.

Self-Question: Is draught a strong wind or a gentle nudge?

Answer: It's more like a gentle but persistent nudge. The pressure differences are actually quite small, often measured in millimeters of water column (mm WC), but crucial for maintaining flow through the entire boiler system!

So, how do we create this all-important airflow? Two main ways:

  • Natural Draught: The Chimney's Magic Trick! This uses the good old "hot air rises" principle. Hot flue gases inside a tall chimney are lighter (less dense) than the cooler, heavier air outside. This density difference creates a natural suction at the chimney base, pulling air through the boiler. The taller the chimney, the stronger the magic!
  • Artificial Draught: When Nature Needs a Boost! For big, modern boilers with lots of twists and turns for flue gases to pass through (like heat recovery devices), natural draught often isn't enough. So, we use fans!
    • Mechanical Draught: Using powerful fans to either push air in or pull gases out.
    • Steam Jet Draught: An older method (think old steam locomotives) where a jet of steam is used to create suction. Not as common in big plants today due to efficiency.

The Power of Hot Air: Natural Draught & Chimney Calculations

How does a chimney "suck" air? It's all about buoyancy! Hot flue gases inside the chimney are less dense than the cooler ambient air outside. This creates a pressure difference that drives the flow.

The theoretical draught produced by a chimney can be expressed as a pressure difference ($\Delta P$) or as an equivalent height of a column of hot gas or ambient air.

Key Formula for Draught Pressure ($\Delta P$):

$\Delta P = H \cdot g \cdot (\rho_a - \rho_g)$
Where:
$H$ = Height of the chimney (m)
$g$ = Acceleration due to gravity ($9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$)
$\rho_a$ = Density of ambient air (kg/m³)
$\rho_g$ = Average density of hot flue gases inside the chimney (kg/m³)

Since density is inversely proportional to absolute temperature (for ideal gases, $\rho = P/(RT)$), we can rewrite this using temperatures:

$\rho_a = \frac{P_a}{R_a T_a}$ and $\rho_g = \frac{P_g}{R_g T_g}$
Assuming $P_a \approx P_g$ (atmospheric pressure) and $R_a \approx R_g$ (gas constant for air and flue gas are similar), the draught pressure is often simplified.
A more common form for draught in terms of **height of water column ($h_w$)**:
$h_w = 353 H \left( \frac{1}{T_a} - \frac{1}{T_g} \cdot \frac{m_a + 1}{m_a} \right)$ mm of water
Where:
$H$ = Height of chimney (m)
$T_a$ = Absolute temperature of ambient air (K)
$T_g$ = Absolute average temperature of flue gases in chimney (K)
$m_a$ = Mass of air supplied per kg of fuel (kg air/kg fuel)
(The term $(m_a+1)/m_a$ accounts for the slightly higher density of flue gas compared to air due to fuel products. If this term is approximated to 1, it simplifies further.)

Self-Question: If my chimney is hotter, will I get more draught?

Answer: Yes, up to a point! Higher $T_g$ makes $\rho_g$ lower, increasing $(\rho_a - \rho_g)$. However, very high $T_g$ also means more heat is wasted up the chimney. There's an optimum $T_g$ for maximum discharge, often around $T_g \approx 2 T_a$.

Calculating Minimum Chimney Height (Conceptual): If you know the total draught required by the boiler system to overcome all resistances ($\Delta P_{required}$ or $h_{w,required}$), you can rearrange the draught formula to estimate the minimum chimney height $H$ needed to produce that natural draught. This often involves knowing the expected flue gas temperature and air-fuel ratio.

The Fan Club: ID, FD, and Balanced Draught Systems

When natural draught isn't enough, we call in the fans!

Induced Draught (ID Fan): The "Puller"

Imagine a giant vacuum cleaner at the chimney base. The ID fan sits *after* the boiler (and any dust collectors/pollution control gear) but *before* the chimney.

  • It SUCKS flue gases out of the furnace and through all the boiler passages.
  • This creates a **negative pressure** (below atmospheric) inside the furnace.
    Why negative? Safety first! If there are any small leaks in the boiler casing, clean air will be sucked IN, rather than hot, toxic flue gases and ash blowing OUT into the boiler room. Much safer for everyone!

Forced Draught (FD Fan): The "Pusher"

This fan is like a powerful blower at the *start* of the system, usually before the air preheater (if one exists) or directly supplying air to the burners.

  • It PUSHES combustion air into the furnace at a pressure *above* atmospheric.
  • If only an FD fan is used, the furnace is "pressurized." This is common in some package boilers.

Typical Balanced Draught System: The Teamwork Approach!

Balanced Draught: The Perfect Teamwork!

Why choose when you can have both? A balanced draught system uses an FD fan to push air in AND an ID fan to pull gases out.

  • They work together like a well-coordinated team.
  • The goal? To keep the furnace pressure *just slightly negative* (e.g., -5 to -10 mm of water column). This gives the safety of an ID system with the good air supply control of an FD system.
  • This is the most common setup for medium to large boilers in power plants and industries.

Self-Question: If the ID fan fails in a balanced draught system, what could happen?

Answer: The FD fan would continue to push air in, making the furnace pressure positive. This could cause hot gases and flames to blow out of inspection doors or other openings – a dangerous situation! That's why safety interlocks are crucial.

Secondary Air Fans (SA Fans): The "Afterburner" Air

Sometimes, the main air supplied with the fuel (primary air) isn't enough for perfect combustion, or we want to control *how* combustion happens. That's where Secondary Air (and sometimes Tertiary Air) comes in, often supplied by separate SA fans.

  • This extra air is injected into the furnace, usually above or around the main flame zone.
  • What does it do?
    • Ensures any unburnt fuel particles or gases get a final chance to burn completely. More efficiency, less smoke!
    • Creates turbulence, mixing the fuel and air better.
    • Can be used strategically for "staged combustion" to reduce the formation of nasty NOx pollutants by creating different burning zones.

Induced Draught (ID) Fan: The Boiler's Exhaust Specialist

An Induced Draught (ID) fan is like the powerful exhaust fan for the entire boiler system. Its main job is to pull the hot, smoky flue gases from the furnace, through all the heat recovery equipment (like superheaters, economizers, air preheaters) and pollution control devices (like ESPs), and then push them up the chimney.

Key Roles:

  • Creates Suction: It maintains a negative pressure (below atmospheric) inside the furnace and flue gas paths. This is a huge safety feature – if there are any leaks, air gets sucked in, rather than hot, toxic gases leaking out.
  • Overcomes Resistance: All those tubes and devices create a lot of resistance to gas flow. The ID fan provides the "pull" needed to overcome this friction.
  • Handles Tough Conditions: ID fans deal with very hot, corrosive, and often dusty gases, so they need to be built tough!
  • Controls Furnace Pressure: In balanced draught systems, it works with the FD fan to keep the furnace pressure just right.

Think of it as the "lungs" exhaling for the boiler, ensuring all the "used air" (flue gas) is safely and effectively removed.

Numerical: Chimney Draught & Air Calculation

Problem: How much air is required per kg of coal in a boiler having chimney height 35 m to create a draught of 20 mm of water? Temperature of flue gas in chimney is 370 °C and boiler house temperature is 34 °C.

Given Data:
  • Chimney height $H = 35 \text{ m}$
  • Draught produced $h_w = 20 \text{ mm of water}$
  • Flue gas temperature $T_g = 370^\circ\text{C} = 370 + 273 = 643 \text{ K}$
  • Boiler house (ambient air) temperature $T_a = 34^\circ\text{C} = 34 + 273 = 307 \text{ K}$
To Find: Mass of air required per kg of coal ($m_a$) Formula for Draught in mm of water:
$h_w = 353 H \left( \frac{1}{T_a} - \frac{1}{T_g} \cdot \frac{m_a + 1}{m_a} \right)$

Solution Steps:

  1. Substitute known values into the formula:
    $20 = 353 \times 35 \left( \frac{1}{307} - \frac{1}{643} \cdot \frac{m_a + 1}{m_a} \right)$
  2. Simplify the equation:

    $20 = 12355 \left( 0.00325739 - \frac{1}{643} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m_a}\right) \right)$

    $\frac{20}{12355} = 0.00325739 - \frac{1}{643} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m_a}\right)$

    $0.00161878 = 0.00325739 - \frac{1}{643} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m_a}\right)$

  3. Isolate the term with $m_a$:

    $\frac{1}{643} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m_a}\right) = 0.00325739 - 0.00161878$

    $\frac{1}{643} \left(1 + \frac{1}{m_a}\right) = 0.00163861$

  4. Solve for $(1 + 1/m_a)$:

    $1 + \frac{1}{m_a} = 0.00163861 \times 643$

    $1 + \frac{1}{m_a} \approx 1.05362$

  5. Solve for $1/m_a$:

    $\frac{1}{m_a} = 1.05362 - 1 = 0.05362$

  6. Solve for $m_a$:
    $m_a = \frac{1}{0.05362} \approx 18.6497 \text{ kg of air / kg of coal}$

Approximately $18.65 \text{ kg}$ of air is required per kg of coal.

MCQ Time! Test Your Draught Knowledge!

1. The "draught" in a boiler system primarily helps to:

  • a) Increase the steam pressure.
  • b) Cool down the furnace walls.
  • c) Cause the flow of air and flue gases.
  • d) Reduce the boiler's fuel consumption directly.

Answer: c) Cause the flow of air and flue gases.

2. In a "Balanced Draught" system, what's the typical pressure condition inside the furnace?

  • a) Highly positive (much above atmospheric).
  • b) Exactly atmospheric pressure.
  • c) Slightly negative (just below atmospheric).
  • d) A very high vacuum.

Answer: c) Slightly negative (just below atmospheric).

3. Which fan in a boiler system gets to handle the hot, often dusty, flue gases?

  • a) Forced Draught (FD) Fan
  • b) Induced Draught (ID) Fan
  • c) Secondary Air (SA) Fan
  • d) Primary Air (PA) Fan (if separate)

Answer: b) Induced Draught (ID) Fan

4. What is the main magic behind "Natural Draught" in a chimney?

  • a) The wind blowing across the chimney top.
  • b) The chimney's material acting like a magnet for smoke.
  • c) Hot flue gases being lighter (less dense) than the cooler outside air.
  • d) A hidden fan inside the chimney base.

Answer: c) Hot flue gases being lighter (less dense) than the cooler outside air.

5. If you want to supply extra air for very complete combustion, especially to burn off any remaining volatiles, you would use a:

  • a) Chimney Damper
  • b) Secondary Air (SA) Fan
  • c) Induced Draught (ID) Fan only
  • d) Steam Jet Blower

Answer: b) Secondary Air (SA) Fan

6. If the height of a chimney producing natural draught is doubled (all other conditions same), the theoretical draught pressure produced will approximately:

  • a) Be halved.
  • b) Remain the same.
  • c) Double.
  • d) Quadruple.

Answer: c) Double. (Since $\Delta P \propto H$)
