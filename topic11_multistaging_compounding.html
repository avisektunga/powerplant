Need for Multi-Staging and Compounding

If the entire pressure drop from boiler pressure to condenser pressure were to occur in a single turbine stage (like a simple De Laval impulse turbine), the steam velocity leaving the nozzles would be extremely high (often supersonic and > 1000 m/s).

For maximum efficiency in a single impulse stage, the optimum blade speed ($U$) is about half the steam jet velocity ($U \approx V_1/2$ for $\alpha_1 \approx 0$, more accurately $U = (V_1 \cos \alpha_1)/2$). This would necessitate impractically high rotor speeds, leading to:

  • Excessive centrifugal stresses in the rotor and blades, potentially causing structural failure.
  • The need for very large reduction gearing if driving standard speed generators (e.g., 3000 or 3600 RPM).
  • Very high frictional losses at such high speeds.
  • If blade speed is kept low relative to such high steam jet velocity, the efficiency would be very poor, and a large amount of kinetic energy would be lost in the exhaust steam (high "leaving loss").

To overcome these issues and to efficiently utilize a large enthalpy (pressure) drop, **multi-staging** and **compounding** techniques are employed.

  • Multi-staging: The overall pressure/enthalpy drop is divided among several stages in series. Each stage handles a smaller portion of the total expansion. This results in lower steam velocities and manageable blade speeds in each stage, leading to higher overall efficiency. Both impulse and reaction turbines are typically multi-staged for power generation.
  • Compounding (specifically for Impulse Turbines): These are methods to reduce the high rotor speed of impulse turbines while utilizing a large enthalpy drop in a more controlled manner, primarily by reducing the steam velocity before it leaves the casing or by absorbing its kinetic energy in steps.

Need for Multi-Staging:

As discussed in the introduction, the primary needs for multi-staging are:

  1. To Manage Rotor Speeds: By dividing the total enthalpy drop into smaller drops per stage, the steam velocity leaving the nozzles (or fixed blades) of each stage is kept at a moderate level. This allows the rotor to operate at practical and safe speeds (e.g., $U \approx V_1/2$ for impulse stages can be achieved without excessive RPM).
  2. To Improve Thermal Efficiency:
    • Operating blades at or near their optimum blade speed ratio ($U/V_1$) for each stage maximizes energy extraction from the steam in that stage.
    • It reduces the kinetic energy of the steam leaving the final stage (known as "leaving loss" or "exhaust loss"), which is a direct loss of available energy.
    • It reduces frictional losses associated with excessively high steam velocities.
  3. To Reduce Centrifugal Stresses: Lower rotor speeds lead to lower centrifugal forces on the blades and rotor disc, allowing for more robust and reliable designs.
  4. To Accommodate Large Volume Flow of Steam: In the lower pressure stages, the specific volume of steam increases significantly. Multi-staging allows for progressively larger blade heights and flow areas to accommodate this volumetric expansion efficiently.

Types of Multi-Staging:

Multi-staging essentially involves arranging multiple single stages in series on a common shaft. Each stage typically consists of a set of stationary blades (nozzles in impulse, fixed guide blades in reaction) and a row of moving blades. The types of multi-staging are based on the type of individual stages used:

  1. Multi-Stage Impulse Turbine: Composed of multiple impulse stages.
    • Pressure Compounding (Rateau Staging): The total pressure drop is divided among multiple simple impulse stages. Each stage consists of a set of nozzles and a row of moving blades. Steam expands only in the nozzles of each stage, and pressure remains relatively constant across the moving blades. The kinetic energy gained in the nozzles is absorbed by the moving blades.
    • Velocity Compounding (Curtis Staging): The steam expands in a single set of nozzles from a high initial pressure to a lower pressure, generating very high velocity. This high-velocity steam then passes through multiple rows of moving blades, with rows of fixed guide blades (which only redirect the steam, without significant pressure drop) in between the moving rows. The kinetic energy is absorbed in steps by the successive rows of moving blades. Typically used for the first stage of a high-pressure turbine to quickly reduce steam temperature and velocity.
    • Pressure-Velocity Compounding: A combination where the total pressure drop is divided into stages (pressure compounding), and one or more of these stages might be velocity-compounded.
  2. Multi-Stage Reaction Turbine (e.g., Parsons Turbine): Composed of multiple reaction stages. In each stage (a pair of fixed and moving rows), steam expands and drops in pressure across both the fixed blades (acting as nozzles) and the moving blades (also acting as nozzles). The enthalpy drop per stage is generally smaller than in impulse stages, requiring a larger number of stages for the same overall pressure drop.
  3. Multi-Stage Impulse-Reaction Turbine: Many modern large turbines use impulse staging (often a velocity-compounded Curtis wheel) at the high-pressure inlet where specific volume is small, followed by reaction staging for the intermediate and low-pressure sections where specific volume is large and reaction blading offers higher efficiency.

Compounding in Steam Turbines

Compounding is a method used primarily in impulse steam turbines to reduce the high rotor speeds that would result from expanding steam in a single stage from boiler pressure to condenser pressure. It involves utilizing the steam's enthalpy drop (and the resulting high velocity) in multiple steps or stages on a single rotor or multiple rotors, allowing for more practical and efficient turbine designs.

The main objective of compounding is to allow the turbine to run at a lower (more practical) rotational speed while maintaining good efficiency by ensuring that the blade speed is appropriately matched to the steam speed in each section where energy is extracted.

1. Velocity Compounding (Curtis Staging)

  • Principle: The total velocity generated by steam expansion in one set of nozzles is absorbed in multiple steps by passing the steam through several rows of moving blades.
  • Arrangement: A velocity-compounded stage consists of:
    1. One set of stationary nozzles where the steam expands from a high pressure to a lower pressure, acquiring very high velocity.
    2. A row of moving blades where a portion of this high kinetic energy is absorbed.
    3. A row of fixed guide blades (reversing blades) that redirect the steam (without significant pressure or velocity drop, ideally) to the next row of moving blades.
    4. Another row of moving blades where more kinetic energy is absorbed.
    This can continue for two or three rows of moving blades (with intervening fixed guide blades). All moving blades are mounted on the same rotor/shaft.
  • Pressure & Velocity Changes:
    • Pressure drops significantly only in the nozzles. It remains nearly constant across all moving and fixed guide blades.
    • Absolute steam velocity is very high after the nozzles and decreases progressively as it passes through each row of moving blades. The fixed guide blades primarily change the direction of steam flow.
  • Advantages: Allows for a large pressure drop (and thus high steam velocity generation) in a single nozzle set while keeping the rotor speed relatively low because the velocity is absorbed in stages. Results in a shorter turbine for a given pressure drop compared to pressure compounding.
  • Disadvantages: Efficiency of later rows of moving blades is lower due to higher frictional losses as the steam has already passed through previous rows. Overall stage efficiency is generally lower than pressure compounding for many stages.
  • Application: Commonly used for the first (governing) stage of high-pressure turbines to quickly reduce steam temperature and velocity, or in small, simple turbines.

2. Pressure Compounding (Rateau Staging)

  • Principle: The total pressure drop of the steam is divided into a series of smaller pressure drops across multiple simple impulse stages. Each stage consists of one set of nozzles and one row of moving blades.
  • Arrangement:
    1. A set of nozzles where a fraction of the total pressure drop occurs, and steam velocity increases.
    2. A row of moving blades where the kinetic energy from the preceding nozzles is absorbed, and work is done. Steam velocity decreases.
    3. This is followed by another set of nozzles and another row of moving blades, and so on.
    All moving blade rows are mounted on the same shaft. The stages are separated by diaphragms which hold the nozzles.
  • Pressure & Velocity Changes:
    • Pressure drops in steps across each set of nozzles. It remains nearly constant across each row of moving blades.
    • Velocity increases in each set of nozzles and decreases as it passes over the subsequent row of moving blades. The velocity entering each nozzle set (after leaving previous moving blades) is relatively low.
  • Advantages: Each stage operates with a more moderate steam velocity, allowing the blade speed to be better matched for good efficiency. The overall efficiency can be higher than velocity compounding if many stages are used.
  • Disadvantages: Requires a larger number of stages (and thus a longer turbine) for the same overall pressure drop compared to a velocity-compounded stage.
  • Application: Widely used in multi-stage impulse turbines.

3. Pressure-Velocity Compounding

This method combines the features of both pressure and velocity compounding. The total pressure drop is divided into several stages (pressure compounding). Each of these stages is then velocity-compounded (i.e., has one set of nozzles and multiple rows of moving blades with fixed guide blades in between).

This approach can achieve a good compromise, allowing for a significant pressure drop per pressure-compounded stage while using velocity compounding within that stage to manage blade speeds. It can result in a turbine that is more compact than a purely pressure-compounded one for a very large overall pressure drop.

MCQ:

1. The primary reason for multi-staging in steam turbines is to:

  • a) Increase the steam inlet pressure.
  • b) Reduce the exhaust steam temperature to absolute zero.
  • c) Operate the turbine at manageable rotor speeds and improve efficiency.
  • d) Simplify the turbine construction.

Answer: c) Operate the turbine at manageable rotor speeds and improve efficiency.

2. In velocity compounding (Curtis stage), steam pressure drops significantly only in the:

  • a) First row of moving blades
  • b) Fixed guide blades
  • c) Nozzles
  • d) All rows of moving blades

Answer: c) Nozzles

3. Pressure compounding (Rateau staging) involves:

  • a) One set of nozzles and multiple rows of moving blades.
  • b) Multiple sets of nozzles, each followed by one row of moving blades.
  • c) Steam expanding in both fixed and moving blades.
  • d) A single stage handling the entire pressure drop.

Answer: b) Multiple sets of nozzles, each followed by one row of moving blades.

4. Which type of compounding is characterized by having fixed guide blades between rows of moving blades?

  • a) Pressure compounding
  • b) Velocity compounding
  • c) Reaction staging
  • d) Reheat staging

Answer: b) Velocity compounding

5. If a large enthalpy drop is handled in a single impulse stage, it leads to:

  • a) Very low steam exit velocity from nozzles.
  • b) Very high steam exit velocity from nozzles and potentially impractical rotor speeds.
  • c) Increased pressure across moving blades.
  • d) Reduced thermal efficiency.

Answer: b) Very high steam exit velocity from nozzles and potentially impractical rotor speeds.

6. The main purpose of fixed guide blades in a velocity-compounded impulse stage is to:

  • a) Expand the steam further to increase its velocity.
  • b) Redirect the steam flow to the next row of moving blades.
  • c) Reduce the pressure of the steam.
  • d) Absorb kinetic energy from the steam.

Answer: b) Redirect the steam flow to the next row of moving blades.
