Turbine Losses

In an ideal steam turbine, the expansion of steam would be isentropic (constant entropy), and all the available enthalpy drop would be converted into useful work. However, in actual turbines, various irreversibilities and practical limitations lead to losses, reducing the actual work output and overall efficiency. These losses can be broadly categorized as internal and external losses.

Internal Losses (Occur within the steam path):

  • Nozzle Friction Loss: Friction between steam and nozzle walls reduces the kinetic energy of steam leaving the nozzle ($V_1$) compared to ideal expansion. This results in a lower enthalpy drop being converted to KE. Represented by nozzle efficiency or velocity coefficient.
  • Blade Friction Loss (Profile Loss): Friction as steam flows over the surface of moving and fixed blades. This converts some kinetic energy into heat, which slightly reheats the steam (increasing entropy and exhaust enthalpy). Represented by blade velocity coefficient.
  • Secondary Flow Losses: Complex three-dimensional flow patterns (e.g., vortices) that occur due to the turning of steam in blade passages and interactions with end walls. These consume energy.
  • Tip Leakage Losses: In reaction turbines (and shrouded impulse blades), there's a pressure difference across the moving blade tips. Steam leaks through the clearance between blade tips and the casing, bypassing the working passage and doing no useful work.
  • Lacing Wire Losses: If lacing wires are used to stiffen long blades, they can cause aerodynamic disturbance and loss.
  • Disc Friction and Windage Loss: Friction between the rotating turbine discs and the surrounding steam, especially significant in high-density steam environments. "Windage" refers to losses due to blades churning relatively stagnant steam in partial admission stages.
  • Wetness Loss: When steam expands into the wet region (below saturation line), moisture droplets form. These droplets, being heavier and moving slower than steam, impinge on blades causing a braking effect and momentum loss. They also affect flow patterns. This loss is significant in low-pressure stages.
  • Incidence Loss (Shock Loss): Occurs if the steam angle at inlet to blades does not match the blade inlet angle, leading to turbulence and separation.

External Losses (Occur outside the main steam path):

  • Gland Leakage Loss (Shaft Seal Leakage): Steam leaking out from the turbine casing where the rotor shaft emerges, or air leaking into the casing in condensing turbines. Labyrinth seals and carbon rings are used to minimize this.
  • Bearing Friction Loss: Mechanical friction in the turbine shaft support bearings, converting shaft work into heat.
  • Governing Valve Pressure Drop Loss (Throttling Loss): When operating at part loads with throttle governing, the pressure drop across the control valves is an irreversible process that reduces available energy.
  • Leaving Loss (Exhaust Loss / Carry-over Loss): The kinetic energy of the steam leaving the last stage of the turbine ($V_{exhaust}^2/2$). This is lost to the condenser (or atmosphere in non-condensing turbines) and represents unutilized energy. This loss can be significant, especially at high loads or with poorly designed exhaust hoods.
  • Radiation and Convection Loss: Heat lost from the hot turbine casing to the surroundings. Generally small for well-insulated large turbines.

The cumulative effect of these losses results in the actual enthalpy drop in the turbine being less than the isentropic enthalpy drop, leading to a lower internal efficiency and overall turbine efficiency.

Blade Erosion in Steam Turbines

Blade erosion is the progressive wearing away of blade material, primarily due to the impact of particles carried by the steam. It is a major concern for turbine reliability and performance, especially in the low-pressure (LP) stages of condensing turbines.

Reasons/Causes of Blade Erosion:

  1. Water Droplet Erosion (Most Common):
    • As steam expands through the LP stages, its temperature and pressure drop, and it eventually crosses the saturation line, becoming wet steam (a mixture of saturated vapor and liquid water droplets).
    • These water droplets, being much denser than the steam, tend to lag behind the steam flow and have different trajectories.
    • The larger, slower-moving droplets are struck by the leading edges of the rapidly rotating blades, or they impinge on the concave surfaces.
    • This repeated high-velocity impact causes mechanical damage, pitting, and removal of blade material. The erosion is most severe on the leading edges and sometimes on the convex side near the trailing edge of the blades in the last few LP stages where wetness is highest.
  2. Solid Particle Erosion (SPE):
    • Caused by hard, abrasive solid particles carried in the steam. These particles can originate from:
      • Exfoliated oxides (magnetite, Fe3O4) from the internal surfaces of boiler tubes, superheater/reheater tubes, and main steam piping, especially during thermal cycling (start-ups/shutdowns).
      • Boiler feedwater contaminants (e.g., silica, iron oxides) that get carried over with steam.
      • Weld slag, rust, or other debris left in the system during construction or maintenance.
    • SPE typically affects the high-pressure (HP) and intermediate-pressure (IP) turbine blades and nozzles more severely, as the particles are introduced with the main steam. It can cause thinning of trailing edges, blunting of leading edges, and general surface wear.
  3. Corrosion-Erosion: A combined effect where a corrosive environment (e.g., due to impurities in steam like chlorides or sulfates) weakens the blade surface, making it more susceptible to mechanical erosion by water droplets or solid particles.

Effects of Blade Erosion:

  • Reduced aerodynamic performance of blades, leading to decreased turbine efficiency.
  • Increased surface roughness, leading to higher friction losses.
  • Changes in blade profile and throat openings, altering steam flow characteristics.
  • Stress concentrations at eroded areas, potentially leading to fatigue cracking and blade failure.
  • Increased vibrations.
  • Reduced operational life and increased maintenance costs.

Prevention and Mitigation of Erosion:

  • For Water Droplet Erosion:
    • Maintaining high superheat and using reheat cycles to delay the onset of wetness.
    • Designing LP stages with features to remove moisture (e.g., internal drains, hollow guide blades).
    • Using erosion-resistant materials for LP blades (e.g., 12% Cr steel, titanium alloys).
    • Applying hardened coatings (e.g., Stellite, plasma spray coatings) or shields to leading edges of vulnerable blades.
    • Optimizing blade shapes and steam velocities.
  • For Solid Particle Erosion:
    • Implementing strict water chemistry control for boiler feedwater to minimize carryover.
    • Chemical cleaning of boiler and steam piping before initial startup and after major overhauls to remove scale and debris.
    • Using steam strainers at turbine inlet.
    • Operational procedures to minimize exfoliation (e.g., controlled start-up/shutdown rates).

Turbine Governing

Turbine governing is the process of controlling the steam flow rate to the turbine to maintain a constant speed under varying loads (for generator drives) or to control the power output or process steam pressure. A governing system senses changes in speed or load and adjusts the steam admission accordingly.

Need for Governing:

  • Speed Control (for Generators): To maintain a constant frequency of the generated electricity (e.g., 50 Hz or 60 Hz), the turbine speed must be kept constant despite load fluctuations on the generator.
  • Load Control: To adjust the power output of the turbine to meet the demand.
  • Pressure Control (for Process Turbines): To maintain a constant exhaust or extraction pressure for industrial processes.
  • Safety: To prevent overspeeding, which can lead to catastrophic failure.

Methods of Governing Steam Turbines:

  1. Throttle Governing:
    • Principle: The steam flow to the turbine is controlled by a single main throttle valve (or a set of synchronously operated valves) located before the first stage nozzles.
    • Operation: To reduce load, the throttle valve is partially closed, which reduces the pressure of steam entering the turbine (throttling process). This reduces the mass flow rate and the available energy per unit mass of steam.
    • Advantages: Simple mechanism, relatively low cost.
    • Disadvantages: Throttling is an irreversible process that reduces the available energy of the steam, leading to significant efficiency losses at part loads (high "throttling loss"). The state line on the h-s diagram shifts due to throttling.
    • Application: Common in smaller turbines or where simplicity is prioritized over part-load efficiency.
  2. Nozzle Control Governing:
    • Principle: The nozzles of the first stage (or several initial stages) are divided into several groups. Each group is supplied with steam through a separate control valve.
    • Operation: To vary the load, these control valves are opened or closed sequentially. At full load, all valves are open. As load decreases, valves are closed one by one, cutting off steam supply to corresponding nozzle groups. The steam supplied to the active nozzle groups still undergoes full expansion.
    • Advantages: More efficient at part loads compared to throttle governing because throttling losses are minimized (only the steam passing through partially open valves, if any, is throttled). Steam to active nozzles expands from nearly full inlet pressure.
    • Disadvantages: More complex and expensive mechanism. Can lead to uneven steam admission and partial admission losses in the first stage if not carefully designed.
    • Application: Common in medium to large turbines where good part-load efficiency is important.
  3. Bypass Governing:
    • Principle: Used to meet overload conditions or to improve efficiency at very high loads.
    • Operation: At normal full load, all steam passes through all turbine stages. For overloads, a bypass valve opens, allowing a portion of the steam from an earlier stage (e.g., after the first few HP stages) to bypass subsequent HP/IP stages and enter directly into a later stage (e.g., LP stages). This increases the total steam flow through the later stages, boosting power output.
    • Advantages: Allows the turbine to handle loads greater than its normal continuous rating for short periods.
    • Disadvantages: Less efficient during bypass operation as some steam does not expand through all stages. More complex system.
  4. Combination Governing: Many large turbines use a combination of methods, e.g., nozzle control for normal load variations and throttle governing for fine adjustments or rapid load changes.

Modern governing systems are typically electro-hydraulic, using electronic controllers to sense speed/load and hydraulic actuators to operate the steam valves for fast and precise control.

MCQ:

1. Which of the following is an internal loss in a steam turbine?

  • a) Bearing friction loss
  • b) Gland leakage loss
  • c) Wetness loss
  • d) Radiation loss from casing

Answer: c) Wetness loss

2. Blade erosion in the low-pressure stages of a steam turbine is primarily caused by:

  • a) High steam temperature
  • b) Impact of solid oxide particles
  • c) Impact of water droplets in wet steam
  • d) Excessive steam velocity

Answer: c) Impact of water droplets in wet steam

3. Throttle governing of a steam turbine controls steam flow by:

  • a) Sequentially opening/closing groups of nozzles.
  • b) Varying the pressure of steam entering the first stage nozzles by a control valve.
  • c) Bypassing steam around certain stages.
  • d) Changing the turbine speed directly.

Answer: b) Varying the pressure of steam entering the first stage nozzles by a control valve.

4. Which governing method generally offers better part-load efficiency for steam turbines?

  • a) Throttle governing
  • b) Nozzle control governing
  • c) Bypass governing
  • d) Emergency trip governing

Answer: b) Nozzle control governing

5. The "Leaving Loss" in a steam turbine refers to:

  • a) Heat lost from the turbine casing to the surroundings.
  • b) Kinetic energy of the steam exiting the last stage.
  • c) Friction losses in the turbine bearings.
  • d) Steam leaking through shaft seals.

Answer: b) Kinetic energy of the steam exiting the last stage.

6. Solid Particle Erosion (SPE) in turbines is often caused by:

  • a) Condensation of steam in LP stages.
  • b) High moisture content in steam.
  • c) Exfoliated oxides from boiler tubes and piping.
  • d) Excessive blade tip clearances.

Answer: c) Exfoliated oxides from boiler tubes and piping.

7. The primary purpose of a turbine governing system when driving an AC generator is to:

  • a) Maximize the steam inlet pressure.
  • b) Maintain a constant turbine speed (and thus generator frequency).
  • c) Minimize blade erosion.
  • d) Increase the vacuum in the condenser.

Answer: b) Maintain a constant turbine speed (and thus generator frequency).
