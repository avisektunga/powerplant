9. Thermodynamics of Compressible Fluid Flow (Nozzles)

What is Compressible Flow? And Why Nozzles?

Imagine trying to squeeze air into a bicycle tire – it gets harder as more air goes in, right? That's because air is **compressible**; its density changes with pressure. When fluids (especially gases like steam or air) move at high speeds, these density changes become super important. This is the realm of compressible flow.

Self-Question: Why don't we usually worry about water being compressible in everyday taps?

Answer: Liquids like water are much less compressible than gases. Their density hardly changes with normal pressure variations. We only consider compressibility for gases at high speeds or large pressure changes.

Now, what's a **nozzle**? Think of it as a specially shaped pipe designed to do one main thing: speed up the fluid passing through it! By accelerating the fluid, we convert its internal thermal energy (or pressure energy) into kinetic energy (energy of motion). This high-speed jet is exactly what we need to spin the blades of a steam turbine.

Key Concepts Revisited:

  • Mach Number (M): $M = V/a$. It's like asking, "How fast is the fluid moving compared to how fast sound travels in it right now?"
    • $M < 1$: Subsonic (slower than sound)
    • $M = 1$: Sonic (at the speed of sound - a special state!)
    • $M > 1$: Supersonic (faster than sound)
  • Speed of Sound (a): $a = \sqrt{\gamma R T}$ for ideal gases. It changes with temperature!
  • Stagnation Properties ($P_0, T_0, h_0$): Imagine stopping the fast-moving fluid smoothly and without losing heat. The pressure, temperature, and enthalpy it would reach are its stagnation properties. In an adiabatic nozzle, stagnation enthalpy ($h_0$) and stagnation temperature ($T_0$ for ideal gas) remain constant!

Types of Nozzles & Their Purpose

Not all nozzles are shaped the same. Their design depends on whether we want to go from slow to fast (subsonic to supersonic) or just make subsonic flow faster.

  1. Convergent Nozzle:

    Its cross-sectional area decreases along the flow direction.
    Function: Excellent for accelerating subsonic flow ($M<1$).
    Limitation: Can only accelerate flow up to sonic velocity ($M=1$) at its exit (the narrowest point, also called the throat if it were part of a C-D nozzle). It cannot produce supersonic flow by itself.

  2. Divergent Nozzle (Supersonic Nozzle):

    Its cross-sectional area increases along the flow direction.
    Function: Used to further accelerate already supersonic flow ($M>1$) to even higher supersonic speeds.
    (Often part of a Convergent-Divergent nozzle)

  3. Convergent-Divergent (C-D) Nozzle / De Laval Nozzle:

    This is the superstar for achieving supersonic speeds! It has a converging section, followed by a narrowest point called the **throat**, and then a diverging section.
    Function:

    • Converging part: Accelerates subsonic inlet flow towards sonic speed.
    • Throat: If conditions are right, flow becomes sonic ($M=1$) here.
    • Diverging part: Accelerates the (now sonic or already supersonic) flow to higher supersonic speeds ($M>1$).

    Self-Question: Why does a diverging section speed up supersonic flow but slow down subsonic flow (acting as a diffuser)?

    Answer: It's all thanks to the Area-Velocity Relationship! $\frac{dA}{A} = \frac{dV}{V} (M^2 - 1)$.
    For $M<1$, $(M^2-1)$ is negative. So, if $dA>0$ (diverging), then $dV$ must be negative (flow slows down).
    For $M>1$, $(M^2-1)$ is positive. So, if $dA>0$ (diverging), then $dV$ must be positive (flow speeds up!). Magic!

Isentropic Flow Through Nozzles - The Ideal Scenario

Let's imagine the perfect nozzle flow: **isentropic**. This means it's reversible (no friction) and adiabatic (no heat exchange with surroundings). Entropy ($s$) stays constant. This is our benchmark!

(Similar to 2025 PYQ) Explain isentropic flow through a nozzle and its characteristics.

Isentropic Flow Through a Nozzle Explained

Isentropic flow is the ideal, frictionless, adiabatic flow where entropy remains constant. In a nozzle, this means the maximum possible kinetic energy is achieved for a given pressure drop.

Key Governing Equations (recap):

  • Continuity: $\rho A V = \text{constant}$
  • Energy (SFEE): $h + \frac{V^2}{2} = h_0 = \text{constant}$
  • Isentropic Relations: $P/\rho^\gamma = \text{const}$, etc. (for ideal gas) or $s=\text{const}$ (general, use steam tables).
  • Area-Velocity Relation: $\frac{dA}{A} = \frac{dV}{V} (M^2 - 1)$

Characteristics of Isentropic Nozzle Flow:

  1. Energy Conversion: Enthalpy (internal + flow energy) is smoothly converted into kinetic energy. Pressure and temperature drop as velocity increases.
  2. Choked Flow & Critical Pressure Ratio:
    • As back pressure ($P_b$) at the nozzle exit is lowered, mass flow rate ($\dot{m}$) increases, up to a maximum value.
    • This maximum $\dot{m}$ occurs when velocity at the throat (narrowest section, or exit for convergent nozzle) reaches sonic speed ($M=1$). This condition is called **choked flow**.
    • The pressure at the throat when $M=1$ is called the **critical pressure ($P^*$**). The ratio $P^*/P_0$ (where $P_0$ is inlet stagnation pressure) is the **critical pressure ratio**.
      For an ideal gas: $\frac{P^*}{P_0} = \left( \frac{2}{\gamma + 1} \right)^{\gamma / (\gamma - 1)}$
      (Approx. 0.528 for air, $\gamma=1.4$; Approx. 0.546 for superheated steam, $\gamma \approx 1.3$)
    • Once choked, further lowering $P_b$ (below $P^*$) does *not* increase $\dot{m}$ or change conditions upstream of the throat.
  3. Operation of C-D Nozzle based on Back Pressure ($P_b$):
    • $P_b / P_0 > P_{design,subsonic}$ (high $P_b$): Flow is subsonic throughout. Nozzle acts as a venturi (accelerates then decelerates).
    • $P_b / P_0 = P_{throat}^* / P_0$: $M=1$ at throat, then flow decelerates subsonically in divergent part (nozzle acts as subsonic diffuser after throat). Choked.
    • $P_{design,supersonic} < P_b / P_0 < P_{throat}^* / P_0$: $M=1$ at throat, flow accelerates to supersonic in divergent part, but a **normal shock wave** occurs inside the divergent section. Across the shock, flow becomes subsonic and pressure rises suddenly. Highly irreversible.
    • $P_b / P_0 = P_{design,supersonic}$: Smooth isentropic acceleration to design supersonic Mach number at exit. This is the **design condition**.
    • $P_b / P_0 < P_{design,supersonic}$ (very low $P_b$): Flow is over-expanded. Supersonic flow exits, then adjusts to $P_b$ via expansion waves outside the nozzle.
  4. Nozzle Efficiency ($\eta_N$): For real nozzles, friction causes losses.
    $\eta_N = \frac{\text{Actual KE at exit}}{\text{Ideal (Isentropic) KE at exit for same pressure drop}} = \frac{V_{2a}^2 / 2}{(V_{2s}^2 / 2) \text{ for same } P_0, P_{exit}}$
    Or based on enthalpy: $\eta_N = \frac{h_0 - h_{2a}}{h_0 - h_{2s}}$ (where $h_{2a}$ is actual exit enthalpy, $h_{2s}$ is isentropic exit enthalpy).
    For isentropic flow, $\eta_N = 100\%$.

Beyond Ideal: Supersaturated Flow & Nozzle Efficiencies

Explain supersaturated expansion in case of flow through nozzle and discuss briefly, the factors causing it. Represent the phenomenon on h-s diagram and state the effects of supersaturation.

Supersaturated Expansion (Metastable Flow) in Nozzles

Imagine steam expanding rapidly in a nozzle. As it crosses the saturated vapor line on an h-s diagram, it *should* start condensing, forming tiny water droplets. But what if it expands *so fast* that it doesn't have time to condense properly at the equilibrium saturation point?

This is **supersaturated flow** (or metastable flow). The steam remains as a vapor even though its temperature has dropped below the saturation temperature corresponding to its pressure. It's like a "supercooled vapor" – existing in a state where it thermodynamically shouldn't!

Self-Question: Is supersaturated steam "drier" than it should be?

Answer: In a way, yes! It hasn't formed the liquid droplets it would have if it were in equilibrium. It's still behaving like a superheated vapor, just in a region where it "should" be wet.

Representation on h-s Diagram (Mollier Chart):

  • The expansion line (e.g., 1-2' on the diagram) continues smoothly past the saturation line (1-2) as if the steam were still superheated or dry.
  • This metastable state persists until a point where condensation suddenly occurs (reversion point), often around the **Wilson Line** (empirically found, typically around 3-5% moisture if equilibrium existed).
  • At reversion, the latent heat released during the sudden condensation causes a slight temperature and entropy increase, bringing the steam state closer to equilibrium (point 2'').

Factors Causing Supersaturation:

  1. High Rate of Expansion: This is the primary cause. If steam expands very rapidly (high velocity, steep pressure gradient, as in a nozzle), the molecules don't have enough time to aggregate and form stable condensation nuclei (tiny droplets). The condensation process has a time lag.
  2. Absence of Nuclei: Condensation prefers to start on existing nuclei (like dust particles or ions). If the steam is very pure and free of such nuclei, the onset of condensation is delayed.
  3. Low Pressure: At lower pressures, the number of molecular collisions is less frequent, further hindering the formation of condensation nuclei.

Effects of Supersaturation:

  • Increased Mass Flow Rate: Because the steam remains as a vapor (higher specific volume than a wet mixture at equilibrium for the same pressure), and behaves like superheated steam with a specific heat ratio $\gamma \approx 1.3$ (instead of $\gamma \approx 1.135$ for wet steam), the density is lower than equilibrium wet steam. For a given throat area and upstream conditions, this results in a slightly higher mass flow rate through the nozzle (typically 1-5% increase) than predicted by equilibrium calculations. This is because $V_{throat} = \sqrt{\gamma P v}$, and with higher specific volume $v$ (as vapor) for supersaturated flow, $V_{throat}$ and $\dot{m}$ can be higher for a given pressure.
  • Reduced Enthalpy Drop (Undercooling Loss): The actual enthalpy drop to the reversion point (1-2') is less than the isentropic enthalpy drop to the equilibrium saturation pressure (1-2). This difference represents a loss, known as undercooling loss, reducing nozzle efficiency.
  • Shift in Dryness Fraction: After reversion, the steam is wetter than if it had expanded in equilibrium to the same pressure.
  • Erosion Potential: The sudden formation of relatively larger droplets at the reversion point can potentially lead to erosion of turbine blades if this point occurs within or near the blading.
  • Nozzle Design Considerations: Nozzle calculations for steam often need to account for supersaturation effects for accurate mass flow and exit condition predictions, especially in HP turbine stages.

Numerical Problems & Solutions

Numerical 1: Heat Drop & Final State in Nozzle (Non-Isentropic)

Problem: Dry steam at 10 bar & 100 m/s enters a nozzle & leaves it with velocity of 300 m/s at 5 bar. For 16 kg/s of steam mass flow rate, determine heat drop in nozzle & final state of steam leaving nozzle, assuming heat loss to surroundings as 10 kJ/kg.

Given Data:
  • Inlet: $P_1 = 10 \text{ bar}$, Dry saturated steam ($x_1=1$), $V_1 = 100 \text{ m/s}$
  • Outlet: $P_2 = 5 \text{ bar}$, $V_2 = 300 \text{ m/s}$
  • Mass flow rate $\dot{m} = 16 \text{ kg/s}$
  • Heat loss $Q_{loss} = -10 \text{ kJ/kg}$ (negative as it's loss from system)
Solution Steps:
  1. Inlet Enthalpy ($h_1$):

    At $P_1 = 10 \text{ bar}$, dry saturated steam ($x_1=1$):
    From steam tables, $h_1 = h_{g @ 10 \text{ bar}} \approx 2776.2 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    (Also, $s_1 = s_{g @ 10 \text{ bar}} \approx 6.5828 \text{ kJ/kgK}$ for later comparison if needed).

  2. Apply Steady Flow Energy Equation (SFEE) between inlet (1) and outlet (2):
    $h_1 + \frac{V_1^2}{2000} + Q = h_2 + \frac{V_2^2}{2000} + W$

    For a nozzle, Work done $W=0$. $Q = Q_{loss} = -10 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $2776.2 + \frac{(100)^2}{2000} + (-10) = h_2 + \frac{(300)^2}{2000}$
    $2776.2 + \frac{10000}{2000} - 10 = h_2 + \frac{90000}{2000}$
    $2776.2 + 5 - 10 = h_2 + 45$
    $2771.2 = h_2 + 45$
    $h_2 = 2771.2 - 45 = 2726.2 \text{ kJ/kg}$.

  3. Heat Drop in Nozzle ($\Delta h$):

    This usually refers to the drop in static enthalpy $(h_1 - h_2)$.
    $\Delta h = h_1 - h_2 = 2776.2 - 2726.2 = \strong{50 \text{ kJ/kg}}$.
    (Note: Sometimes "heat drop" can refer to the isentropic enthalpy drop, but here it's interpreted as actual static enthalpy change or available energy converted.) The question likely means the actual enthalpy drop $(h_1-h_2)$. The energy converted to KE is $(h_1-h_2)+Q_{loss} = V_2^2/2000 - V_1^2/2000 = 40 kJ/kg$. So $h_1-h_2 = 40 - Q_{loss} = 40 - (-10) = 50 kJ/kg$. This matches.)

  4. Final State of Steam ($x_2$ or $T_{sup2}$):

    At outlet: $P_2 = 5 \text{ bar}$, $h_2 = 2726.2 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    From steam tables at $P_2 = 5 \text{ bar}$:
    $h_{f @ 5 \text{ bar}} \approx 640.1 \text{ kJ/kg}$
    $h_{g @ 5 \text{ bar}} \approx 2747.5 \text{ kJ/kg}$
    Since $h_f < h_2 < h_g$ ($640.1 < 2726.2 < 2747.5$), the steam is wet.
    $h_2 = h_{f @ 5 \text{ bar}} + x_2 \cdot h_{fg @ 5 \text{ bar}}$
    $h_{fg @ 5 \text{ bar}} = h_g - h_f = 2747.5 - 640.1 = 2107.4 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    $2726.2 = 640.1 + x_2 \cdot (2107.4)$
    $x_2 \cdot (2107.4) = 2726.2 - 640.1 = 2086.1$
    $x_2 = \frac{2086.1}{2107.4} \approx 0.9899$.

Heat drop in nozzle ($h_1 - h_2$) = $50 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
Final state of steam: Wet steam at 5 bar with dryness fraction $x_2 \approx 0.99$.

Numerical 2: C-D Nozzle Discharge Velocity (Isentropic)

Problem: A convergent divergent adiabatic steam nozzle is supplied with steam at 10 bar and 250°C. The discharge pressure is 1.2 bar. Assuming that the nozzle efficiency is 100% (i.e., isentropic flow) and initial velocity of steam is 50 m/s. Find the discharge velocity.

Given Data:
  • Inlet (1): $P_1 = 10 \text{ bar}$, $T_1 = 250^\circ\text{C}$, $V_1 = 50 \text{ m/s}$
  • Outlet (2): $P_2 = 1.2 \text{ bar}$
  • Nozzle efficiency $\eta_N = 100\%$ (Isentropic flow, $s_2 = s_1$)
  • Adiabatic flow ($Q=0$)
Solution Steps:
  1. Inlet Enthalpy ($h_1$) and Entropy ($s_1$):

    At $P_1 = 10 \text{ bar}$, $T_1 = 250^\circ\text{C}$ (Superheated steam, since $T_{sat @ 10 bar} \approx 179.9^\circ\text{C}$).
    From superheated steam tables:
    $h_1 \approx 2943.1 \text{ kJ/kg}$
    $s_1 \approx 6.9263 \text{ kJ/kgK}$

  2. Outlet Enthalpy ($h_{2s}$) for Isentropic Expansion:

    Since flow is isentropic, $s_2 = s_1 \approx 6.9263 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    At $P_2 = 1.2 \text{ bar}$:
    From saturated steam tables at $P_2 = 1.2 \text{ bar}$:
    $s_{f @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} \approx 1.3606 \text{ kJ/kgK}$
    $s_{g @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} \approx 7.2976 \text{ kJ/kgK}$
    Since $s_f < s_2 < s_g$, the steam at outlet is wet.
    Dryness fraction $x_2$: $s_2 = s_{f @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} + x_2 \cdot s_{fg @ 1.2 \text{ bar}}$
    $s_{fg @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} = s_g - s_f = 7.2976 - 1.3606 = 5.937 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    $6.9263 = 1.3606 + x_2 \cdot (5.937)$
    $x_2 \cdot (5.937) = 6.9263 - 1.3606 = 5.5657$
    $x_2 = \frac{5.5657}{5.937} \approx 0.9375$.

    Now find $h_{2s}$ (isentropic outlet enthalpy):
    $h_{f @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} \approx 439.4 \text{ kJ/kg}$
    $h_{fg @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} \approx 2244.1 \text{ kJ/kg}$ (Note: $h_g - h_f$)
    $h_{2s} = h_{f @ 1.2 \text{ bar}} + x_2 \cdot h_{fg @ 1.2 \text{ bar}}$
    $h_{2s} = 439.4 + 0.9375 \times 2244.1 = 439.4 + 2103.84 \approx 2543.24 \text{ kJ/kg}$.

  3. Apply SFEE to find Discharge Velocity ($V_2$):
    $h_1 + \frac{V_1^2}{2000} = h_{2s} + \frac{V_2^2}{2000}$ (since adiabatic, $Q=0$, and no work, $W=0$)

    $2943.1 + \frac{(50)^2}{2000} = 2543.24 + \frac{V_2^2}{2000}$
    $2943.1 + \frac{2500}{2000} = 2543.24 + \frac{V_2^2}{2000}$
    $2943.1 + 1.25 = 2543.24 + \frac{V_2^2}{2000}$
    $2944.35 = 2543.24 + \frac{V_2^2}{2000}$
    $\frac{V_2^2}{2000} = 2944.35 - 2543.24 = 401.11$
    $V_2^2 = 401.11 \times 2000 = 802220$
    $V_2 = \sqrt{802220} \approx 895.67 \text{ m/s}$.

Discharge velocity $V_2 \approx 895.67 \text{ m/s}$.

MCQ:

1. The continuity equation for steady, one-dimensional compressible flow states that:

  • a) $\rho A V^2 = \text{constant}$
  • b) $\rho A V = \text{constant}$
  • c) $A V / \rho = \text{constant}$
  • d) $P + \frac{1}{2}\rho V^2 = \text{constant}$

Answer: b) $\rho A V = \text{constant}$

2. For subsonic compressible flow ($M < 1$) through a nozzle, to increase the velocity, the area must:

  • a) Increase (divergent)
  • b) Decrease (convergent)
  • c) Remain constant
  • d) First increase then decrease

Answer: b) Decrease (convergent)

3. In a convergent-divergent (De Laval) nozzle, sonic velocity ($M=1$) is typically achieved at the:

  • a) Inlet section
  • b) Convergent section
  • c) Throat (minimum area)
  • d) Divergent section exit

Answer: c) Throat (minimum area)

4. If a convergent nozzle is "choked," it means:

  • a) The flow has stopped completely.
  • b) The mass flow rate is maximum for the given inlet conditions and cannot be increased by further lowering back pressure.
  • c) The exit velocity is supersonic.
  • d) The nozzle is blocked by foreign particles.

Answer: b) The mass flow rate is maximum for the given inlet conditions and cannot be increased by further lowering back pressure.

5. For supersonic compressible flow ($M > 1$) through a nozzle, to further increase the velocity, the area must:

  • a) Increase (divergent)
  • b) Decrease (convergent)
  • c) Remain constant
  • d) First decrease then increase

Answer: a) Increase (divergent)

6. The stagnation enthalpy ($h_0$) in an adiabatic nozzle flow:

  • a) Increases along the flow direction.
  • b) Decreases along the flow direction due to acceleration.
  • c) Remains constant throughout the nozzle.
  • d) Is equal to the static enthalpy at the throat.

Answer: c) Remains constant throughout the nozzle. (This is from SFEE: $h + V^2/2 = h_0 = \text{constant}$ for adiabatic flow with no work.)

7. The critical pressure ratio ($P^*/P_0$) for a nozzle depends primarily on:

  • a) The nozzle inlet temperature only.
  • b) The nozzle length and exit area.
  • c) The specific heat ratio ($\gamma$) of the fluid.
  • d) The ambient atmospheric pressure.

Answer: c) The specific heat ratio ($\gamma$) of the fluid.

8. Supersaturated flow in a steam nozzle occurs because:

  • a) The steam is heated above its saturation temperature.
  • b) Condensation is delayed due to rapid expansion.
  • c) The nozzle has a very rough surface finish.
  • d) The steam contains a high percentage of non-condensable gases.

Answer: b) Condensation is delayed due to rapid expansion.

9. The Wilson line on an h-s diagram represents:

  • a) The path of isentropic compression.
  • b) The limit of stable superheating.
  • c) The locus of points where rapid condensation begins in supersaturated expansion.
  • d) The critical pressure line.

Answer: c) The locus of points where rapid condensation begins in supersaturated expansion.

10. Nozzle efficiency primarily accounts for losses due to:

  • a) Heat transfer to the surroundings.
  • b) Friction within the nozzle.
  • c) Incomplete combustion upstream of the nozzle.
  • d) Changes in potential energy.

Answer: b) Friction within the nozzle.
