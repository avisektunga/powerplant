Introduction to Steam Turbines

A steam turbine is a rotary mechanical device that extracts thermal energy from pressurized steam and converts it into useful rotary motion. This rotary motion is then typically used to drive an electrical generator to produce electricity, or to power machinery like pumps and compressors.

The basic principle involves directing high-velocity jets of steam onto a series of blades attached to a rotor, causing the rotor to spin. The steam expands and cools as it passes through the turbine stages, giving up its energy to the blades.

Classification of Steam Turbines

Steam turbines can be classified based on various criteria:

  1. Based on the Principle of Steam Action:
    • Impulse Turbine: The expansion of steam and the resulting pressure drop occur mainly in stationary nozzles. The high-velocity steam jets from the nozzles impinge on the moving blades, causing them to rotate due to the change in momentum (impulse force). Pressure across the moving blades remains relatively constant. Example: De Laval turbine, Curtis turbine (velocity compounded).
    • Reaction Turbine: Steam expansion and pressure drop occur in both stationary (fixed) blades and moving blades. The moving blades are shaped like nozzles, causing further expansion and acceleration of steam relative to the blades. The rotation is caused by both impulse effect (due to entering steam) and reaction effect (due to accelerating steam leaving the blades). Example: Parsons turbine.
    • Impulse-Reaction Turbine: Most modern large turbines are a combination, typically with impulse stages at the high-pressure end and reaction stages at the low-pressure end.
  2. Based on the Direction of Steam Flow:
    • Axial Flow Turbine: Steam flows along the axis of the turbine shaft. Most common type for power generation.
    • Radial Flow Turbine: Steam flows radially outwards or inwards. Example: Ljungstrom turbine (a type of reaction turbine). Tangential flow (Helical flow) turbines also exist but are less common.
  3. Based on the Number of Stages:
    • Single-Stage Turbine: Expansion of steam from inlet to exhaust pressure occurs in a single stage (one set of nozzles and blades). Typically used for small power applications or as auxiliary drives. Example: De Laval turbine.
    • Multi-Stage Turbine: Steam expansion is divided into multiple stages to improve efficiency and manage blade speeds. Each stage consists of a set of nozzles/fixed blades and moving blades. Most power plant turbines are multi-stage.
  4. Based on Steam Inlet/Exhaust Conditions:
    • Condensing Turbine: Exhaust steam pressure is below atmospheric pressure (vacuum), maintained by a condenser. Maximizes enthalpy drop and efficiency. Common in power plants.
    • Non-Condensing (Back-Pressure) Turbine: Exhaust steam pressure is above atmospheric pressure. The exhaust steam is used for process heating (cogeneration).
    • Extraction (Pass-Out) Turbine: Steam is extracted from one or more intermediate stages at specific pressures for process use or feedwater heating, while the remaining steam continues to expand to the exhaust.
    • Reheat Turbine: Steam is extracted after partial expansion in the HP turbine, returned to the boiler for reheating, and then readmitted to the IP/LP turbine.
  5. Based on Type of Compounding (for Impulse Turbines):
    • Pressure Compounding (Rateau staging)
    • Velocity Compounding (Curtis staging)
    • Pressure-Velocity Compounding
  6. Based on Casing Arrangement:
    • Single Casing
    • Tandem Compound (Multiple casings driving a single generator on one shaft)
    • Cross Compound (Multiple casings driving separate generators or on parallel shafts)

Working Principle of a Single-Stage Impulse Turbine:

The fundamental principle of an impulse turbine is the conversion of the kinetic energy of a high-velocity fluid jet into mechanical work by changing the direction (and hence momentum) of the fluid flow.

  1. Nozzle Action: High-pressure steam from the boiler is expanded in one or more stationary nozzles. In the nozzles, the pressure energy of the steam is converted into kinetic energy, resulting in a high-velocity jet of steam. The pressure of the steam drops significantly as it passes through the nozzle, but its velocity increases substantially.
  2. Impulse on Blades: This high-velocity steam jet is then directed onto a series of curved blades mounted on the periphery of a rotor.
  3. Change in Momentum: As the steam flows over the curved surface of the blades, its direction of velocity changes. This change in momentum exerts an impulse force on the blades.
  4. Rotor Rotation: This tangential force on the blades creates a torque on the rotor, causing it to rotate and produce mechanical work.
  5. Constant Pressure Across Moving Blades (Ideal): Ideally, there is no pressure drop across the moving blades.

Velocity Diagram (Simplified for a single stage):

Velocity diagrams represent steam velocities relative to nozzles, blades, and casing.

  • $V_1$: Absolute velocity of steam leaving nozzle (angle $\alpha_1$).
  • $U$: Mean blade velocity.
  • $V_{r1}$: Relative velocity of steam entering moving blades (angle $\beta_1$).
  • $V_{r2}$: Relative velocity of steam leaving moving blades (angle $\beta_2$). Ideally $|V_{r2}| = |V_{r1}|$. With friction, $|V_{r2}| = k \cdot |V_{r1}|$.
  • $V_2$: Absolute velocity of steam leaving moving blades (angle $\alpha_2$).
  • $V_{w1}, V_{w2}$: Velocities of whirl (tangential components of $V_1, V_2$).
  • $V_{f1}, V_{f2}$: Velocities of flow (axial components of $V_1, V_2$).

Work done per unit mass = $U \cdot (V_{w1} \pm V_{w2})$ or $U \cdot \Delta V_w$.

Detailed velocity triangles are crucial for analysis. See Topic 10 for more.

Blade Efficiency and Degree of Reaction - Derivations

Understanding the efficiency of turbine stages involves analyzing the energy transfer from the steam to the blades. This often starts with Euler's turbine equation and velocity diagrams.

Euler's Turbine Equation (Work Done per unit mass):

The work done ($W$) by the steam on the rotor per unit mass of steam is given by the change in the moment of momentum of the steam as it passes through the rotor:

$W = U_1 V_{w1} - U_2 V_{w2}$ (for radial flow)
For axial flow turbines, where the mean blade speed $U$ is often assumed constant ($U_1 = U_2 = U$):
$W = U (V_{w1} - V_{w2})$
(Note: The sign of $V_{w2}$ depends on its direction. If $V_{w2}$ is in the opposite direction to $U$ or $V_{w1}$, it becomes $U(V_{w1} + |V_{w2}|)$. A more general form using $\Delta V_w$ (change in whirl velocity) is $W = U \cdot \Delta V_w$.)
Blade Efficiency (or Diagram Efficiency, $\eta_b$):

Blade efficiency is the ratio of the work done on the blades to the kinetic energy supplied to the blades (or the energy available for work in that stage).

$\eta_b = \frac{\text{Work done on blades per unit mass}}{\text{Energy supplied to blades per unit mass}}$

For an impulse stage, the energy supplied is primarily the kinetic energy of steam leaving the nozzle, $\frac{1}{2}V_1^2$.

$\eta_b (\text{impulse}) = \frac{U (V_{w1} - V_{w2})}{\frac{1}{2}V_1^2}$
Maximum Efficiency of a Single-Stage Impulse Turbine (De Laval):

Consider a single impulse stage with symmetrical blades ($\beta_1 = \beta_2$) and frictionless flow over blades ($V_{r1} = V_{r2}$).

From inlet velocity triangle: $V_{w1} = V_1 \cos \alpha_1$. $V_{r1}^2 = V_1^2 + U^2 - 2UV_1 \cos \alpha_1$.

From outlet velocity triangle: $V_{w2} = U - V_{r2} \cos \beta_2 = U - V_{r1} \cos \beta_1$.

For symmetrical blades, $\beta_1 = \beta_2$. The relative velocity component $V_{r1} \cos \beta_1$ can be expressed as $(V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)$.

So, $V_{w2} = U - (V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U) = 2U - V_1 \cos \alpha_1$.

Work done, $W = U(V_{w1} - V_{w2}) = U[V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - (2U - V_1 \cos \alpha_1)] = U[2V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - 2U] = 2U(V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)$.

Blade efficiency, $\eta_b = \frac{2U(V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)}{\frac{1}{2}V_1^2} = \frac{4U(V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2}$.

Let blade speed ratio $\rho = U/V_1$. Then $\eta_b = 4\rho(\cos \alpha_1 - \rho)$.

To find maximum efficiency, differentiate $\eta_b$ with respect to $\rho$ and set to zero: $\frac{d\eta_b}{d\rho} = 4(\cos \alpha_1 - 2\rho) = 0$.

This gives the optimum blade speed ratio: $\rho_{opt} = \frac{U}{V_1} = \frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}$.

Substituting $\rho_{opt}$ back into the efficiency equation:

$\eta_{b,max} = 4 \left(\frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}\right) \left(\cos \alpha_1 - \frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}\right) = 2 \cos \alpha_1 \left(\frac{\cos \alpha_1}{2}\right) = \cos^2 \alpha_1$

Thus, the maximum blade efficiency for a single-stage impulse turbine with symmetrical blades and no friction is $\cos^2 \alpha_1$, occurring when $U = \frac{V_1 \cos \alpha_1}{2}$.

Degree of Reaction (R):

The degree of reaction is defined as the ratio of enthalpy drop (or pressure drop) in the moving blades to the total enthalpy drop (or pressure drop) in the stage (nozzles + moving blades).

$R = \frac{\text{Enthalpy drop in moving blades}}{\text{Enthalpy drop in the stage (fixed + moving blades)}}$

Alternatively, $R = \frac{\Delta h_{moving}}{\Delta h_{fixed} + \Delta h_{moving}}$.

Using steam velocities, it can also be expressed (for an axial flow turbine, assuming $V_{f1}=V_{f2}$ for 50% reaction):

$R = \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{V_1^2 - V_2^2 + V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}$ (This is a general form)

A more common form using enthalpy changes from the steady flow energy equation:

Enthalpy drop in moving blades: $h_1' - h_2' = \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{2}$ (where $h'$ refers to static enthalpy across moving blades).

Enthalpy drop in fixed blades/nozzles: $h_0 - h_1 = \frac{V_1^2 - V_0^2}{2}$ (where $V_0$ is inlet velocity to nozzle, often small).

Total enthalpy drop in stage $\approx \frac{V_1^2}{2} + \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{2}$ (if $V_0$ is negligible and considering KE changes).

Simplified, focusing on KE changes as drivers for enthalpy drop: $R = \frac{\text{KE gained due to expansion in moving blades}}{\text{KE gained in nozzles} + \text{KE gained in moving blades}}$

  • For a pure Impulse Turbine (De Laval type), ideally $R=0$, as all enthalpy drop occurs in the nozzles, and $V_{r1} = V_{r2}$ (no expansion in moving blades).
  • For a pure Reaction Turbine (like Parsons turbine with 50% reaction), $R=0.5$. This means enthalpy drop is equally shared between fixed and moving blades. For 50% reaction turbines, often $\alpha_1 = \beta_2$ and $\alpha_2 = \beta_1$, and $V_1 = V_{r2}$ and $V_2 = V_{r1}$.
Condition for Maximum Efficiency of a 50% Reaction Turbine Stage (Parsons Turbine):

For a 50% reaction stage with symmetrical blading (i.e., fixed and moving blades have same shape, so $\alpha_1 = \beta_2$ and $\beta_1 = \alpha_2$), and assuming $V_1 = V_{r2}$ and $V_2 = V_{r1}$.

Work done $W = U(V_{w1} + V_{w2})$ (since $V_{w2}$ is usually in the opposite direction for reaction blades if $\alpha_2 > 90^\circ$).

$V_{w1} = V_1 \cos \alpha_1$. $V_{w2} = V_2 \cos \alpha_2 = V_{r1} \cos \beta_1$.

From inlet triangle: $V_{r1} \cos \beta_1 = V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U$.
From outlet triangle (for 50% reaction, often $\alpha_2 = 90^\circ$, so $V_{w2}=0$ is an ideal target for axial discharge, but not always assumed for general efficiency):
If we assume $V_{w2} = U - V_{r2}\cos\beta_2 = U - V_1\cos\alpha_1$ (this form is when $\beta_2$ angle definition makes $V_{r2}\cos\beta_2$ point forward with U)

Using the common relation for 50% reaction: $W = U(2V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)$.

Energy input to the stage per unit mass = Enthalpy drop in stage = $\Delta h_{stage} = \Delta h_{fixed} + \Delta h_{moving}$.
For 50% reaction, $\Delta h_{fixed} = \frac{V_1^2 - V_2^2}{2}$ (if $V_2$ is inlet to next fixed blade, usually exit velocity of previous moving blade)
and $\Delta h_{moving} = \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{2}$.
So, $\Delta h_{stage} = \frac{V_1^2 - V_2^2}{2} + \frac{V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2}{2}$.
For 50% reaction with $V_1=V_{r2}$ and $V_2=V_{r1}$: $\Delta h_{stage} = V_1^2 - V_1 U \cos \alpha_1 + U^2/2$ (This is a more involved derivation using blade angles).

A simpler approach for blade efficiency considers kinetic energy input to moving blades and KE generated within them:
Energy supplied (considering changes in KE for the stage) = $V_1^2 - U V_1 \cos\alpha_1 + U^2 \cos^2\alpha_1$ (This is complex, derived from specific velocity triangle relations for 50% reaction).

The simplified common result for blade efficiency of a 50% reaction stage is:
$\eta_b = \frac{2U(2V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2 + (V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)^2 + U^2}$ ... this path is getting too specific to triangle setups.

More directly for 50% reaction, $\eta_b = \frac{U(2V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2 - (V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - U)^2}$.
Optimum blade speed ratio $\rho = U/V_1 = \cos \alpha_1$.

Substituting this into the work done $W = V_1 \cos \alpha_1 (2V_1 \cos \alpha_1 - V_1 \cos \alpha_1) = V_1^2 \cos^2 \alpha_1$.

The energy input for a 50% reaction stage can be shown to be $V_1^2(1 - \frac{\cos^2 \alpha_1}{2})$ under specific assumptions.

The maximum blade efficiency for a 50% reaction stage is often given as:

$\eta_{b,max} = \frac{2 \cos^2 \alpha_1}{1 + \cos^2 \alpha_1}$
This occurs when the blade speed ratio $U/V_1 = \cos \alpha_1$.

Note: These derivations can be quite involved and depend on specific assumptions about blade angles and friction. The above are common simplified results.

MCQ:

1. In an impulse turbine, the primary expansion of steam occurs in the:

  • a) Moving blades only
  • b) Stationary nozzles only
  • c) Both nozzles and moving blades equally
  • d) Condenser

Answer: b) Stationary nozzles only

2. Which type of turbine typically requires more stages for the same overall pressure drop?

  • a) Impulse turbine
  • b) Reaction turbine
  • c) Both require the same number of stages
  • d) Single-stage De Laval turbine

Answer: b) Reaction turbine (as pressure drop per stage is smaller for optimal efficiency)

3. The blades of a reaction turbine are shaped like:

  • a) Symmetrical cups
  • b) Flat plates
  • c) Aerofoils or nozzles
  • d) Helical screws

Answer: c) Aerofoils or nozzles

4. A turbine that exhausts steam at a pressure below atmospheric is called a:

  • a) Back-pressure turbine
  • b) Pass-out turbine
  • c) Condensing turbine
  • d) Topping turbine

Answer: c) Condensing turbine

5. In an ideal impulse turbine stage, the pressure of steam as it passes over the moving blades:

  • a) Increases significantly
  • b) Decreases significantly
  • c) Remains relatively constant
  • d) Fluctuates unpredictably

Answer: c) Remains relatively constant

6. The De Laval turbine is an example of a:

  • a) Single-stage reaction turbine
  • b) Multi-stage impulse turbine
  • c) Single-stage impulse turbine
  • d) Radial flow turbine

Answer: c) Single-stage impulse turbine

7. Higher axial thrust is generally a characteristic of which type of turbine, requiring balancing arrangements?

  • a) Impulse turbine
  • b) Reaction turbine
  • c) Both experience similar axial thrust
  • d) Helical flow turbine

Answer: b) Reaction turbine

8. For a single-stage impulse turbine with nozzle angle $\alpha_1$, the maximum blade efficiency $\eta_{b,max}$ is:

  • a) $\sin^2 \alpha_1$
  • b) $\cos \alpha_1$
  • c) $\cos^2 \alpha_1$
  • d) $(1 + \cos \alpha_1) / 2$

Answer: c) $\cos^2 \alpha_1$

9. The degree of reaction (R) for a pure impulse turbine stage is ideally:

  • a) 0
  • b) 0.5
  • c) 1
  • d) Greater than 1

Answer: a) 0

10. For a 50% reaction turbine (Parsons turbine), the optimum blade speed ratio ($U/V_1$) for maximum efficiency is generally:

  • a) $\sin \alpha_1$
  • b) $\cos \alpha_1 / 2$
  • c) $\cos \alpha_1$
  • d) $2 \cos \alpha_1$

Answer: c) $\cos \alpha_1$
