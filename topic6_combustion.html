Igniting the Power: Introduction to Combustion Systems

What's the secret sauce that makes a boiler churn out all that powerful steam? It's **Combustion!** Think of it as a very carefully controlled, super-intense fire. It's a rapid chemical dance between a fuel (like coal, oil, or gas) and an oxidant (usually oxygen from the air), releasing a tremendous amount of heat and light.

The goal of a boiler's combustion system isn't just to make a big fire, but to do it smartly:

  • Get every last bit of energy out of the fuel (maximize heat release).
  • Burn it completely – no wasting fuel or making too much soot and nasty carbon monoxide (CO).
  • Keep the bad stuff (pollutants like NOx, SOx) to a minimum.
  • Provide a steady, controllable flame that can adjust to how much steam is needed.
  • And of course, do all this safely!

Self-Question: Why is "complete combustion" so important?

Answer: Two big reasons! 1) Efficiency: If fuel doesn't burn completely, you're wasting its energy potential (like leaving unpopped kernels in a popcorn bag). 2) Pollution: Incomplete combustion creates harmful pollutants like carbon monoxide (CO) and soot (particulate matter).

Fuel Feast: Different Ways to Burn Different Fuels

Boilers are like chefs; they have different ways to "cook" (burn) different types of fuel.

Solid Fuel Showdown (Coal, Wood, Biomass)

  • Grate Firing: The Classic Barbecue Style
    Solid chunks of fuel sit on a grate (like a barbecue grill). Air comes from below (and sometimes above) to help it burn.
    Think: Old-school coal furnaces or wood-burning stoves.
    Pro Tip: Mechanical stokers automate the fuel feeding – no more shoveling coal by hand for big boilers! Chain grates, spreader stokers are examples.
  • Pulverized Fuel (PF) Combustion: The "Exploding Dust" Method!
    Coal is ground into a super-fine powder (like flour or talcum powder!). This dust is blown into the furnace with air and burns almost instantly while suspended, like a flammable cloud.
    Think: A controlled dust explosion, releasing massive heat very quickly.
    Why it's cool: Super efficient, responds fast to changes in steam demand. Used in most big power plants.
  • Fluidized Bed Combustion (FBC): The "Hot Tub" for Fuel!
    Imagine a bed of hot sand or ash with air blowing up through it, making the particles dance and swirl like boiling water – that's a fluidized bed! Fuel particles (even tricky ones like low-grade coal or waste) are fed into this "hot tub" and burn very efficiently.
    Think: A bubbling, churning hot tub of fire!
    Superpowers: Can burn almost anything! Lower temperatures mean less NOx pollution. Plus, you can add limestone to the bed to capture sulfur (SOx) right there in the furnace!

Liquid & Gaseous Fuel Fiesta: Smooth Operators

  • Burners: The Precision Mixers
    For liquid fuels (like oil), burners atomize it – spray it into incredibly tiny droplets. More surface area means faster burning!
    For gaseous fuels (like natural gas), burners mix the gas thoroughly with air for a clean, efficient flame.
    Think: A high-tech spray nozzle for liquid fuels, or a perfectly designed gas stove burner for gaseous fuels.
  • Advantages: Usually easier to handle, control, and burn cleanly compared to solids.

Decoding Coal: Proximate vs. Ultimate Analysis

To understand how well coal will burn and what its impact will be, engineers analyze its composition in two main ways. Think of it as getting a "quick check-up" versus a "full medical report" for the coal.

What do you understand by proximate and ultimate analysis of coal? Explain briefly with practical significance. (Combines Q1 & Q2a)

Proximate Analysis: The Quick Snapshot

Proximate analysis gives a quick overview of the coal's main components by weight percentage. It's like a basic ID card for the coal.

It determines four things:

  1. Moisture Content: The amount of water present in the coal.

    Self-Question: Why is there water in coal? Isn't coal dry?

    Answer: Coal often has inherent moisture trapped within its structure and surface moisture from mining or storage.

  2. Volatile Matter (VM): These are the substances in coal (like gases and tars) that are driven off as a vapor when coal is heated in the absence of air. Think of them as the "easy-to-burn" flammable gases.
  3. Fixed Carbon (FC): This is the solid combustible material left behind after the volatile matter is driven off. It's mostly carbon, but can contain some hydrogen, sulfur, and nitrogen not released with VM. This is the "char" that burns more slowly on the grate or as a particle.
  4. Ash: The inorganic, non-combustible residue left after the coal is completely burned. It's the "leftovers" that don't provide heat.

Practical Significance of Proximate Analysis:

  • Fuel Ranking & Pricing: Helps classify coal (e.g., lignite, bituminous, anthracite) and determine its commercial value. Higher fixed carbon and lower ash/moisture are generally better.
  • Combustion System Design:
    • High VM coal ignites easily and burns with a long flame – good for pulverized fuel systems.
    • Low VM coal (like anthracite) is harder to ignite and needs specific grate/furnace designs.
    • High ash content means more ash handling equipment is needed and can lead to slagging/fouling problems.
    • High moisture means lower effective heating value and potential ignition problems.
  • Boiler Efficiency Estimation: Moisture and ash reduce the useful energy content.

Ultimate Analysis: The Detailed Chemical Breakdown

Ultimate analysis dives deeper, giving the elemental composition of the coal by weight percentage, usually on a dry, ash-free basis to compare the combustible part itself. It's like a detailed DNA test for the coal.

It determines the percentage of:

  • Carbon (C)
  • Hydrogen (H)
  • Sulphur (S)
  • Nitrogen (N)
  • Oxygen (O) (often by difference)
  • Ash content is also reported as part of the full analysis.

Practical Significance of Ultimate Analysis:

  • Calorific Value Calculation: Allows for more accurate calculation of the coal's heating value (e.g., using Dulong's formula).
  • Stoichiometric Air Calculation: Essential for determining the theoretical amount of air required for complete combustion (important for fan sizing and combustion control).
  • Flue Gas Analysis: Helps predict the composition and quantity of flue gases produced.
  • Pollutant Emission Estimation:
    • Sulphur content directly relates to SOx emissions.
    • Nitrogen content contributes to NOx emissions (fuel NOx).
  • Boiler Design: Influences furnace volume, heat transfer surface design, and emission control equipment selection.

The Difference in a Nutshell (Q2a):

Proximate analysis tells you about the *groups* of substances (moisture, volatiles, fixed carbon, ash). Ultimate analysis tells you about the fundamental *elements* (C, H, S, N, O, Ash).

Coal's Quirks: Moisture, Sulphur & Volatiles in Action

Effects of Moisture, Sulphur, and Volatile Matter in Coal Combustion (Addresses 2018 Q2, and new Q2b,c,d)

Coal isn't just "coal"; its specific characteristics dramatically change how it behaves in a boiler.

(Q2b) How does the percentage moisture affect the quality of coal?

Moisture is generally an unwelcome guest in coal!

  • Energy Thief: Moisture doesn't burn. Instead, it steals heat from the fire just to evaporate and then get superheated with the flue gases. This "stolen" heat goes up the chimney, lowering the boiler's efficiency.
    Think: Trying to boil a pot of water with a wet log. Some energy first goes into drying the log before it can properly burn and heat the water.
  • Lower Effective Heating Value: You pay for the weight of water in the coal, but it gives you no energy. So, the useful energy per kg of "as-received" coal is lower.
  • Ignition Problems: Very damp coal is harder to light and can make the flame unstable.
  • Handling Headaches: Wet, fine coal can be sticky and clog up conveyors, bunkers, and pulverizers.
  • More Flue Gas: The evaporated moisture adds to the flue gas volume, meaning fans might have to work harder.

So, generally, lower moisture content = better quality coal for combustion.

(Q2c) How does sulphur occur in coal?

Sulphur sneaks into coal in a few different forms:

  1. Pyritic Sulphur: This is sulphur combined with iron, primarily as iron pyrite (FeS2 – also known as "fool's gold"). These are distinct mineral particles mixed within the coal. Some of this can be removed by coal cleaning/washing processes.
  2. Organic Sulphur: This sulphur is chemically bonded as part of the complex organic molecules that make up the coal itself. It's much harder to remove before combustion.
  3. Sulphate Sulphur: Usually present in smaller amounts, as mineral sulphates like calcium sulphate (gypsum) or iron sulphate. This form is generally stable and doesn't contribute much to SOx emissions during combustion but adds to the ash content.

Self-Question: If sulphur burns, does it add to the heat produced?

Answer: Yes, a little bit! Sulphur is combustible. But the problems it causes (pollution, corrosion) usually far outweigh this small energy contribution.

(Q2d) What is the effect of volatile matter in the combustion process? (Also covers part of 2018 Q2)

Volatile Matter (VM) is like the "flashy" part of the coal – the gases and tars that get released quickly when coal is heated.

  • Easy Ignition: High VM coals light up much more easily because these flammable gases are released at lower temperatures.
    Think: Trying to light a piece of paper (high VM) vs. a dense piece of charcoal (low VM). The paper catches fire instantly!
  • Flame Characteristics: High VM coals produce long, luminous (bright yellow/orange) flames because the volatiles burn as gases. Low VM coals (like anthracite) burn with shorter, less luminous flames, more like glowing embers.
  • Combustion Rate: The volatiles burn off relatively quickly. The remaining fixed carbon (char) burns more slowly.
  • Furnace Design:
    • Long flames from high VM coals need larger furnace volumes and good air mixing to ensure complete combustion of the volatiles before they hit cooler surfaces (which could cause soot).
    • Low VM coals might need special ignition support or hotter furnace conditions.
  • Smoke & Soot Potential: If there isn't enough air or good mixing when the volatiles are released, they can burn incompletely, producing smoke and soot.
  • Suitability for Systems:
    • High VM bituminous coals are excellent for pulverized coal (PF) firing because they ignite readily in suspension.
    • Low VM anthracite is often burned on specific types of grates (stokers).

So, volatile matter content is a key indicator of how a coal will ignite and burn, influencing the choice of combustion technology and furnace design.

Key Combustion Concepts: The Science of Fire!

Let's get a bit more technical. Understanding these terms is key to mastering combustion!

  • Stoichiometry: The Perfect Recipe! This is the chemistry of calculating the *exact* ideal amount of air (oxidant) needed to completely burn a given amount of fuel. No leftovers, no shortages!
  • Air-Fuel Ratio (A/F): How Much Air per Bite of Fuel? The mass of air supplied divided by the mass of fuel.
    • Stoichiometric A/F Ratio: The perfect ratio from our stoichiometric recipe.
    • Excess Air: The "Just In Case" Air. In real life, we add a bit more air than theoretically needed (e.g., 15-30% extra). Why? To make absolutely sure every last bit of fuel finds oxygen and burns completely.

      Self-Question: Is more excess air always better?

      Answer: Nope! Too little = smoky, incomplete combustion (bad!). Too much = you're just heating up extra air and sending it up the chimney, which wastes energy and lowers efficiency (also bad!). Finding the "sweet spot" is key.

  • Heating Value (CV): Fuel's Energy Punch! How much heat is released when fuel burns completely.
    • HHV (Higher/Gross): Assumes water vapor from combustion condenses and we get its latent heat back (like a "total" energy value).
    • LHV (Lower/Net): Assumes water vapor stays as gas and its latent heat is lost up the chimney (often more realistic for boiler calculations).
  • Combustion Efficiency: The Furnace's Report Card. What percentage of the fuel's energy is actually released as heat in the furnace? Unburnt fuel or CO means lower efficiency.

Numerical Problem: Boiler Heat Balance

Numerical: Heat Balance & Boiler Efficiency Calculation (Coal Fired) (Addresses Q3)

Problem: Coal of calorific value 28000 kJ/kg fired in a boiler generates 8 kg of steam per kg of coal at 35 bar and 350°C (original problem stated 830°C, using 350°C as a more typical superheat value for this CV; if 830°C is firm, recalculate $h_s$). Temperature of feed water is 38°C. The products of combustion per kg of coal are 18.75 kg of gases with 0.75 kg of moisture (this is total moisture in flue gas). Boiler house temperature = 27°C, Flue gas temperature = 121°C. Partial pressure of moisture in flue gas may be assumed to be 0.07 bar. Specific heat of *dry* flue gases ($C_{pg}$) = 1.005 kJ/kgK. Draw up a heat balance per kg of coal burnt and find the thermal efficiency of the boiler.

Given Data (per kg of coal):

  • Calorific Value of Coal (CV) = 28000 kJ/kg
  • Steam generated ($\dot{m}_s / \dot{m}_f$, here $m_s$) = 8 kg/kg coal
  • Steam condition: P = 35 bar, T = 350°C
  • Feedwater temperature ($T_{fw}$) = 38°C
  • Mass of total flue gases ($m_{g,total}$) = 18.75 kg/kg coal
  • Mass of moisture in flue gases ($m_{w,gas}$) = 0.75 kg/kg coal
  • Boiler house temperature (Ambient temp, $T_a$) = 27°C = 300 K
  • Flue gas temperature ($T_g$) = 121°C = 394 K
  • Partial pressure of moisture in flue gas ($p_w$) = 0.07 bar
  • Specific heat of dry flue gases ($C_{pg}$) = 1.005 kJ/kgK

Step 1: Heat Input from Fuel (per kg of coal)

Heat input = CV = 28000 kJ

Step 2: Determine Enthalpies for Steam Calculation

  • $h_{fw}$ (Enthalpy of feedwater at 38°C):
    From steam tables, $h_f \text{ at } 38^\circ\text{C} \approx 159.19 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  • $h_s$ (Enthalpy of superheated steam at 35 bar, 350°C):
    From superheated steam tables, $h_s \approx 3100.8 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
    (Note: If actual problem meant $T_s = 830^\circ\text{C}$, $h_s$ would be much higher, e.g., approx. $4200-4300 \text{ kJ/kg}$ depending on tables. This would drastically change efficiency.)

Step 3: Heat Absorbed by Steam (Useful Heat Output, $Q_{useful}$)

$Q_{useful} = m_s \times (h_s - h_{fw})$

$Q_{useful} = 8 \text{ kg/kg coal} \times (3100.8 - 159.19) \text{ kJ/kg}$
$Q_{useful} = 8 \times 2941.61 = \strong{23532.88 \text{ kJ/kg coal}}$

Step 4: Calculate Heat Losses (per kg of coal)

  1. Loss due to dry flue gases ($Q_{drygas}$):

    Mass of dry flue gases ($m_{dg}$) = $m_{g,total} - m_{w,gas} = 18.75 - 0.75 = 18 \text{ kg/kg coal}$.

    $Q_{drygas} = m_{dg} \times C_{pg} \times (T_g - T_a)$

    $Q_{drygas} = 18 \times 1.005 \times (121 - 27) = 18 \times 1.005 \times 94 = \strong{1699.86 \text{ kJ/kg coal}}$

  2. Loss due to moisture in flue gases ($Q_{moisture\_loss}$):

    This $m_{w,gas}$ (0.75 kg/kg coal) is the total water vapor formed (from fuel's H2, fuel's inherent moisture, and air's humidity) and carries away its sensible and latent heat.

    Enthalpy of superheated steam (moisture) in flue gas at $T_g=121^\circ\text{C}$ and $p_w=0.07 \text{ bar}$:
    $T_{sat} \text{ at } 0.07 \text{ bar} \approx 39^\circ\text{C}$. Since $T_g (121^\circ\text{C}) > T_{sat} (39^\circ\text{C})$, the moisture is superheated.
    $h_{steam@Tg,pw} \approx h_{g@0.07bar} + C_{p,steam}(T_g - T_{sat@0.07bar})$
    From steam tables, $h_{g@0.07bar} \approx 2571.6 \text{ kJ/kg}$. Assume $C_{p,steam} \approx 2.0 \text{ kJ/kgK}$.
    $h_{steam@Tg,pw} \approx 2571.6 + 2.0 \times (121 - 39) = 2571.6 + 164 = 2735.6 \text{ kJ/kg}$.

    Enthalpy of water at ambient temperature ($T_a=27^\circ\text{C}$): $h_{water@Ta} \approx h_{f@27C} \approx 113.16 \text{ kJ/kg}$.

    $Q_{moisture\_loss} = m_{w,gas} \times (h_{steam@Tg,pw} - h_{water@Ta})$

    $Q_{moisture\_loss} = 0.75 \times (2735.6 - 113.16) = 0.75 \times 2622.44 = \strong{1966.83 \text{ kJ/kg coal}}$

  3. Loss due to radiation, convection, and unaccounted ($Q_{unaccounted}$):
    $Q_{unaccounted} = \text{Heat Input} - (Q_{useful} + Q_{drygas} + Q_{moisture\_loss})$

    $Q_{unaccounted} = 28000 - (23532.88 + 1699.86 + 1966.83)$
    $Q_{unaccounted} = 28000 - 27199.57 = \strong{800.43 \text{ kJ/kg coal}}$

Step 5: Heat Balance Sheet (per kg of coal)

Step 6: Thermal Efficiency of the Boiler ($\eta_{th}$)

$\eta_{th} = \frac{Q_{useful}}{\text{Heat Input}} \times 100\% = \frac{23532.88}{28000} \times 100\% \approx 84.05\%$

The thermal efficiency of the boiler is approximately 84.05%.

MCQ Time! Fire Up Your Brain Cells!

1. If coal has a high "volatile matter" content, it will generally:

  • a) Be very hard to ignite.
  • b) Produce a lot of ash.
  • c) Ignite easily and burn with a long flame.
  • d) Have a very low heating value.

Answer: c) Ignite easily and burn with a long flame.

2. Which type of combustion system involves grinding coal into a fine powder before burning it in suspension?

  • a) Grate Firing
  • b) Fluidized Bed Combustion (FBC)
  • c) Pulverized Fuel (PF) Combustion
  • d) Underfeed Stoker

Answer: c) Pulverized Fuel (PF) Combustion

3. Adding limestone to a Fluidized Bed Combustor primarily helps to:

  • a) Increase the coal's heating value.
  • b) Reduce NOx emissions by lowering temperature.
  • c) Capture SOx emissions.
  • d) Make the ash easier to handle.

Answer: c) Capture SOx emissions.

4. What does "stoichiometric air-fuel ratio" mean?

  • a) The maximum amount of air that can be supplied.
  • b) The minimum air needed for ignition.
  • c) The chemically perfect amount of air for complete combustion with no air left over.
  • d) The ratio when using 50% excess air.

Answer: c) The chemically perfect amount of air for complete combustion with no air left over.

5. Which coal analysis gives the percentage of Carbon, Hydrogen, Sulphur, Nitrogen, and Oxygen?

  • a) Proximate Analysis
  • b) Ultimate Analysis
  • c) Sieve Analysis
  • d) Ash Fusion Test

Answer: b) Ultimate Analysis

6. The Lower Heating Value (LHV) of a fuel is different from its Higher Heating Value (HHV) because LHV:

  • a) Accounts for the heat recovered from condensing water vapor in flue gases.
  • b) Does NOT account for the latent heat of vaporization of water formed during combustion.
  • c) Is always higher than HHV.
  • d) Is used only for gaseous fuels.

Answer: b) Does NOT account for the latent heat of vaporization of water formed during combustion.

7. In pulverized fuel combustion, the primary air is mainly used for:

  • a) Cooling the furnace walls.
  • b) Transporting the pulverized fuel to the burners and initial combustion.
  • c) Completing the combustion of unburnt particles above the flame.
  • d) Atomizing liquid fuel.

Answer: b) Transporting the pulverized fuel to the burners and initial combustion.

8. Which of the following conditions would most likely lead to incomplete combustion and CO formation?

  • a) Very high percentage of excess air.
  • b) Insufficient air supply or poor mixing of fuel and air.
  • c) High furnace temperature.
  • d) Using fuel with low volatile matter.

Answer: b) Insufficient air supply or poor mixing of fuel and air.
