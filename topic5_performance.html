5. Boiler Performance Analysis and Heat Balance

Boiler Efficiency (η)

Boiler efficiency is the ratio of the heat output (heat absorbed by the working fluid - water/steam) to the heat input (heat supplied by the fuel).

General Formula:

η = (Heat absorbed by water/steam) / (Heat supplied by fuel) × 100%

More specifically:

η = (ṁs × (hs - hfw)) / (ṁf × CVf) × 100%

Where:

  • η (eta) = Boiler efficiency
  • ṁs = Mass flow rate of steam (kg/s or kg/hr)
  • hs = Specific enthalpy of steam at boiler outlet (kJ/kg)
  • hfw = Specific enthalpy of feedwater at boiler inlet (kJ/kg)
  • ṁf = Mass flow rate of fuel (kg/s or kg/hr)
  • CVf = Calorific Value of the fuel (kJ/kg)

Equivalent Evaporation (from and at 100°C)

It is a standard measure to compare the capacity of different boilers. It represents the amount of water that would be evaporated from feedwater at 100°C to saturated steam at 100°C by the same amount of heat that the boiler actually supplies to the water/steam under operating conditions.

Formula:

Equivalent Evaporation (ṁe) = (ṁs × (hs - hfw)) / hfg @ 100°C

Where:

  • ṁe = Equivalent evaporation rate (kg/s, kg/hr, or kg/kg of fuel)
  • hfg @ 100°C = Latent heat of vaporization of water at 100°C (typically 2257 kJ/kg).
  • Other terms are as defined for efficiency.

It is often expressed in kg of steam "from and at 100°C" per kg of fuel, or per hour.

Boiler Heat Balance Sheet

A heat balance sheet is an account of all the heat energy supplied by the fuel and how it is distributed (utilized and lost). It helps identify areas of heat loss and potential for efficiency improvements. The total heat input must equal the total heat output (utilized + losses).

Heat Supplied (Input): Typically based on 1 kg of fuel.

  1. Heat from combustion of fuel = CVf of fuel (kJ/kg of fuel)

Heat Utilized & Lost (Output): Per kg of fuel.

  1. Heat absorbed by water to form steam (Useful Heat):
    Quseful = ms × (hs - hfw)
    (Here ms is kg of steam per kg of fuel)
  2. Heat loss due to dry flue gases:
    Qdrygas = mg × Cpg × (Tg - Ta)
    (mg = mass of dry flue gas/kg fuel, Cpg = specific heat of dry gas, Tg = flue gas temp, Ta = ambient air temp)
  3. Heat loss due to moisture in fuel:
    Qmoist_fuel = mmoisture_in_fuel × [hsup.steam @ Tg, Ppartial - hwater @ Ta]
  4. Heat loss due to water from combustion of hydrogen in fuel (H2):
    QH2O_comb = mH2O_from_H2 × [hsup.steam @ Tg, Ppartial - hwater @ Ta]
    (mH2O_from_H2 = 9 × mass of H2 per kg fuel)
  5. Heat loss due to moisture in combustion air:
    Qmoist_air = mair × humidityair × [hsup.steam @ Tg, Ppartial - hsteam @ Ta, Ppartial]
  6. Heat loss due to incomplete combustion (CO in flue gas):
    QCO = (mCO × CVCO)
    (mCO = mass of CO/kg fuel, CVCO = calorific value of CO to CO2)
  7. Heat loss due to unburnt carbon in ash/refuse:
    Qunburnt_C = munburnt_C_in_ash × CVCarbon
  8. Heat loss due to radiation and convection from boiler surfaces (Unaccounted Losses): Typically estimated or found by difference.
    Qrad_conv = Heat Input - Σ (All other Utilized & Lost Heat)

A detailed heat balance sheet is usually presented in a table format, showing each item as kJ/kg of fuel and as a percentage of the total heat supplied.

Previous Year Questions & Numericals:

2018 Q7: Heat balance & efficiency of boiler.

Boiler efficiency has been defined above. A heat balance involves accounting for all energy inputs and outputs for a boiler.

Boiler Efficiency (η):
η = (Heat Output in Steam) / (Heat Input from Fuel) × 100%

Heat Balance Sheet Purpose:

  • To determine the boiler efficiency by the indirect method (calculating losses).
  • To identify and quantify individual heat losses.
  • To pinpoint areas for potential improvement in boiler operation and design to reduce losses and increase efficiency.
  • To compare performance under different operating conditions or with different fuels.

Procedure for Heat Balance (typically per kg of fuel or per hour):

  1. Calculate Heat Input: This is primarily the calorific value of the fuel multiplied by the amount of fuel burned. (e.g., Qin = ṁf × CVf).
  2. Calculate Heat Output (Useful Heat Absorbed by Steam): This is the heat absorbed by the feedwater to become steam at the outlet conditions (e.g., Qout = ṁs × (hsteam - hfeedwater)).
  3. Calculate Major Heat Losses:
    • Loss due to dry flue gas (sensible heat).
    • Loss due to moisture from H2 in fuel (latent and sensible heat of water vapor).
    • Loss due to moisture already present in fuel.
    • Loss due to moisture in combustion air.
    • Loss due to incomplete combustion (e.g., CO formation).
    • Loss due to unburnt carbon in ash and refuse.
    • Loss due to radiation, convection, and other unaccounted factors (often found by difference).
  4. Tabulate the Balance: Present the heat input, useful heat output, and all losses in both absolute energy units (e.g., kJ/kg of fuel or kW) and as a percentage of the total heat input. The sum of useful heat output and all losses should equal the total heat input.

The direct method of efficiency calculation is simpler (Qout / Qin). The indirect method (100% - Sum of % Losses) is often more accurate for detailed performance analysis as it highlights specific loss areas.

(Similar to 2025 Q7b) Numerical: Equivalent Evaporation Calculation

Problem: 8 kg of steam is produced at 14 bar and 0.95 dryness in a boiler fed with water at 39°C, per kg of coal consumed. Determine the equivalent evaporation from and at 100°C.

Given Data:

  • Mass of steam produced per kg of coal (ms/mf) = 8 kg steam / kg coal
  • Steam pressure (P) = 14 bar
  • Steam dryness fraction (x) = 0.95
  • Feedwater temperature (Tfw) = 39°C
  • Latent heat of vaporization at 100°C (hfg@100) = 2257 kJ/kg (standard value)

Step 1: Determine Enthalpy of Feedwater (hfw)

hfw = Specific enthalpy of water at 39°C.

Using approximation: hfw ≈ Cp,water × Tfw = 4.186 kJ/kg°C × 39°C ≈ 163.25 kJ/kg.

(From steam tables, hf at 39°C is approximately 163.36 kJ/kg. We'll use this.)

hfw = 163.36 kJ/kg

Step 2: Determine Enthalpy of Steam Produced (hs)

At 14 bar, from steam tables for saturated steam:

  • hf (enthalpy of saturated liquid) ≈ 830.1 kJ/kg
  • hfg (enthalpy of evaporation) ≈ 1957.7 kJ/kg

Enthalpy of wet steam (hs) = hf + x × hfg

hs = 830.1 + (0.95 × 1957.7) = 830.1 + 1859.815 = 2689.915 kJ/kg

Step 3: Calculate Equivalent Evaporation (me) per kg of coal

me = ( (ms/mf) × (hs - hfw) ) / hfg@100°C

me = ( 8 kg steam/kg coal × (2689.915 kJ/kg - 163.36 kJ/kg) ) / 2257 kJ/kg

me = ( 8 × 2526.555 ) / 2257 kg steam (f&a 100°C) / kg coal

me = 20212.44 / 2257 ≈ 8.955 kg of steam (from & at 100°C) per kg of coal.

Answer: The equivalent evaporation is approximately 8.955 kg of steam (from and at 100°C) per kg of coal.

MCQ:

1. The unit of "Equivalent Evaporation" is typically:

  • a) kg (or kg/hr, or kg/kg of fuel)
  • b) kJ/kg
  • c) Unitless
  • d) kW

Answer: a) kg (or kg/hr, or kg/kg of fuel) (It represents a mass of steam).

2. Boiler efficiency is defined as the ratio of:

  • a) Heat input from fuel to heat output in steam
  • b) Heat output in steam to heat input from fuel
  • c) Mass of steam generated to mass of fuel consumed
  • d) Actual evaporation to equivalent evaporation

Answer: b) Heat output in steam to heat input from fuel

3. The value 2257 kJ/kg in the context of boiler calculations typically represents:

  • a) Specific heat of water
  • b) Calorific value of a standard fuel
  • c) Latent heat of vaporization of water at 0°C
  • d) Latent heat of vaporization of water at 100°C

Answer: d) Latent heat of vaporization of water at 100°C

4. Which of the following losses is usually the largest in a conventional boiler's heat balance?

  • a) Loss due to unburnt carbon in ash
  • b) Loss due to radiation and convection
  • c) Loss due to moisture in fuel
  • d) Loss due to dry flue gases (stack loss)

Answer: d) Loss due to dry flue gases (stack loss)

5. A boiler produces 10 kg of steam per kg of fuel. If the factor of evaporation is 1.1, what is the equivalent evaporation per kg of fuel?

  • a) 9.09 kg
  • b) 10 kg
  • c) 11 kg
  • d) 1.1 kg

Answer: c) 11 kg (Factor of Evaporation = (hs - hfw) / hfg@100. Equivalent Evaporation = Actual Evaporation × Factor of Evaporation)

Additional Numerical Problems (Thermodynamic Cycles)

The following numerical problems relate more broadly to thermodynamic power cycles which utilize boilers. They often require the use of Mollier charts or detailed steam tables.

Numerical: Reheat Cycle Analysis

Problem: In a reheat cycle, the initial steam pressure and the maximum temperature are 150 bar and 550 °C respectively. If the condenser pressure is 0.1 bar and dryness fraction is 0.95 at condenser inlet then determine (i) Reheat Pressure, (ii) The cycle efficiency, (iii) The steam rate. Use Mollier diagram (or steam tables).

This problem requires thermodynamic cycle analysis using steam tables or a Mollier chart. A full step-by-step solution involves plotting points on a T-s or h-s diagram and calculating enthalpies at various states.

Approach:

  1. State 1 (Boiler Outlet/HP Turbine Inlet): P1 = 150 bar, T1 = 550 °C. Find h1, s1.
  2. State 4 (Condenser Inlet): P4 = 0.1 bar, x4 = 0.95. Find h4, s4.
  3. State 2 (HP Turbine Outlet/Reheater Inlet): To find the reheat pressure (P2), we assume isentropic expansion in the LP turbine from state 3 (reheater outlet) to state 4. Since T3 (reheat temp) is usually same as T1, we need to work backward or iterate for P2.
    Alternatively, if the problem implies the expansion from state 1 to state 2 (HP exhaust) and then reheat to state 3 (T3=T1), and then expansion from 3 to 4. The problem is phrased such that we need to find P2 such that after reheating to T3=550°C (at P2=P3) and expanding to 0.1 bar, x4=0.95. This is an iterative process if s3 is not equal to s1 (which it won't be necessarily).
    A common simplification or assumption if not enough data is given: The reheat occurs at an optimal pressure (often ~20-25% of initial pressure), or it's chosen such that moisture at LP exhaust is limited. Here, the condition x4=0.95 *defines* the end state of LP expansion.
  4. Finding Reheat Pressure (Preheat = P2 = P3):
    • At P4 = 0.1 bar, find sf4 and sg4. Calculate s4 = sf4 + x4sfg4.
    • Assume isentropic expansion in LP turbine: s3 = s4.
    • State 3 is: T3 = 550 °C, s3 (known). From superheated steam tables, find P3 (this is Preheat) and h3.
    • (i) Reheat Pressure = P3.
  5. State 2 (HP Turbine Outlet): Assume isentropic expansion in HP turbine: s2 = s1. At P2 (=P3) and s2, find h2.
  6. State 5 (Condenser Outlet): P5 = 0.1 bar, saturated liquid. Find h5 = hf @ 0.1 bar.
  7. State 6 (Pump Outlet/Boiler Inlet): P6 = 150 bar. h6 = h5 + Wp. Pump work Wp = vf5(P6-P5).
  8. Calculations:
    • Turbine Work (WT) = (h1-h2) + (h3-h4)
    • Heat Supplied (Qin) = (h1-h6) + (h3-h2) (main boiler + reheater)
    • (ii) Cycle Efficiency (ηcycle) = (WT - Wp_total) / Qin (or Wnet / Qin)
    • (iii) Steam Rate (SR) = 3600 / Wnet (kg/kWh), where Wnet is in kJ/kg.

Note: Performing these calculations requires detailed steam table lookups and potentially interpolation. For example (approximate values):
P1=150 bar, T1=550°C: h1≈3448.6 kJ/kg, s1≈6.5197 kJ/kgK.
P4=0.1 bar, x4=0.95: hf4≈191.8 kJ/kg, hfg4≈2392.1 kJ/kg => h4≈191.8 + 0.95*2392.1 ≈2464.3 kJ/kg.
sf4≈0.6492, sfg4≈7.4995 => s4≈0.6492 + 0.95*7.4995 ≈7.7737 kJ/kgK.
So, s3 = 7.7737 kJ/kgK. At T3=550°C and s3=7.7737, find P3 from superheated tables. This P3 is Preheat. Then proceed.

A full numerical solution requires access to steam tables or software for precise values. The steps above outline the methodology.

Numerical: Rankine Cycle with Efficiencies

Problem: A Rankine cycle operates between pressure of 80 bar and 0.1 bar. The maximum cycle temperature is 600 °C. If the steam turbine and condensate pump efficiencies are 0.9 and 0.8 respectively, calculate (i) specific work, (ii) heat supplied in the boiler, and (iii) thermal efficiency. Assume ideal process elsewhere.

Approach:

  1. State 1 (Turbine Inlet): P1 = 80 bar, T1 = 600 °C. Find h1, s1 from superheated steam tables.
  2. State 2s (Ideal Turbine Outlet): P2 = 0.1 bar, s2s = s1 (isentropic expansion). Find x2s (dryness fraction at ideal outlet) and then h2s = hf@0.1bar + x2s × hfg@0.1bar.
  3. State 2a (Actual Turbine Outlet): Turbine efficiency ηT = (h1 - h2a) / (h1 - h2s) = 0.9.
    Calculate actual enthalpy drop: (h1 - h2a) = ηT × (h1 - h2s).
    So, h2a = h1 - ηT × (h1 - h2s).
  4. State 3 (Pump Inlet/Condenser Outlet): P3 = 0.1 bar, saturated liquid. Find h3 = hf@0.1bar and v3 = vf@0.1bar.
  5. State 4s (Ideal Pump Outlet): P4 = 80 bar. Ideal pump work Wps = v3 × (P4 - P3).
    h4s = h3 + Wps.
  6. State 4a (Actual Pump Outlet/Boiler Inlet): Pump efficiency ηP = Wps / Wpa = 0.8.
    Actual pump work Wpa = Wps / ηP.
    h4a = h3 + Wpa.
  7. Calculations:
    • (i) Specific Work Output (Wnet):
      Actual Turbine Work (WTa) = h1 - h2a.
      Wnet = WTa - Wpa.
    • (ii) Heat Supplied in Boiler (Qin):
      Qin = h1 - h4a.
    • (iii) Thermal Efficiency (ηth):
      ηth = Wnet / Qin.

Example values (approximate from tables/calculator):
P1=80 bar, T1=600°C: h1≈3642 kJ/kg, s1≈6.998 kJ/kgK.
P2=0.1 bar: hf≈191.8, hfg≈2392.1, sf≈0.6492, sg≈8.1488.
s2s=s1=6.998. x2s = (6.998-0.6492)/(8.1488-0.6492) ≈ 0.846.
h2s ≈ 191.8 + 0.846*2392.1 ≈ 2212.5 kJ/kg.
h2a = 3642 - 0.9*(3642-2212.5) = 3642 - 0.9*1429.5 = 3642 - 1286.55 = 2355.45 kJ/kg.
WTa = 3642 - 2355.45 = 1286.55 kJ/kg.
P3=0.1 bar: h3=hf≈191.8 kJ/kg, v3=vf≈0.001010 m³/kg.
Wps = 0.001010 * (80-0.1)*100 kPa = 0.001010 * 79.9 * 100 ≈ 8.07 kJ/kg. (Note: 1 bar = 100 kPa)
Wpa = 8.07 / 0.8 = 10.09 kJ/kg.
h4a = 191.8 + 10.09 = 201.89 kJ/kg.
(i) Specific work (Wnet) = 1286.55 - 10.09 = 1276.46 kJ/kg.
(ii) Heat supplied (Qin) = 3642 - 201.89 = 3440.11 kJ/kg.
(iii) Thermal efficiency (ηth) = 1276.46 / 3440.11 ≈ 0.3710 or 37.10%.

Again, precise values require detailed steam tables. The methodology is key.
