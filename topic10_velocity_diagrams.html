Introduction to Velocity Diagrams

Velocity diagrams (or velocity triangles) are graphical vector representations crucial for analyzing steam flow through turbine stages. They depict the absolute and relative velocities of steam at the inlet and outlet of both stationary (nozzles/fixed blades) and moving blades.

Key Velocity Components (Axial Flow Turbine):

  • $U$: Mean blade velocity (peripheral speed of the rotor). $U = \frac{\pi D N}{60}$.
  • $V_1$: Absolute velocity of steam at inlet to moving blades (exit from nozzles/fixed blades).
  • $\alpha_1$: Angle of $V_1$ with the direction of blade motion $U$ (Nozzle angle).
  • $V_{r1}$: Relative velocity of steam at inlet to moving blades ($\vec{V_{r1}} = \vec{V_1} - \vec{U}$).
  • $\beta_1$: Angle of $V_{r1}$ (Moving blade inlet angle).
  • $V_{r2}$: Relative velocity of steam at outlet from moving blades.
  • $\beta_2$: Angle of $V_{r2}$ (Moving blade outlet angle).
  • $V_2$: Absolute velocity of steam at outlet from moving blades ($\vec{V_2} = \vec{V_{r2}} + \vec{U}$).
  • $\alpha_2$: Angle of $V_2$.
  • $V_{w1}, V_{w2}$: Tangential (Whirl) components of $V_1, V_2$. ($V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1$, $V_{w2} = V_2 \cos\alpha_2$).
  • $V_{f1}, V_{f2}$: Axial (Flow) components of $V_1, V_2$. ($V_{f1} = V_1 \sin\alpha_1$, $V_{f2} = V_2 \sin\alpha_2$).

Work Done per unit mass (Euler's Equation): $W = U(V_{w1} \pm V_{w2})$. The sign depends on the direction of $V_{w2}$. More generally, $W = U \Delta V_w$.
Power Developed $P = \dot{m} W$, where $\dot{m}$ is the mass flow rate of steam.

Blade (Diagram) Efficiency $\eta_b = \frac{\text{Work Done on Blades}}{\text{Energy Supplied to Blades}}$.

Impulse Turbine Velocity Diagrams & Performance

In an ideal impulse turbine stage, steam expansion occurs only in the nozzles, and pressure remains constant across the moving blades. The moving blades are typically symmetrical ($\beta_1 = \beta_2$).

Work Done & Blade Efficiency for Impulse Turbine:

For symmetrical blades ($\beta_1 = \beta_2$) and considering blade friction ($k = V_{r2}/V_{r1}$, where $k$ is blade velocity coefficient):

$V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1$

From inlet triangle: $V_{r1} \cos\beta_1 = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U$

From outlet triangle: $V_{w2} = U - V_{r2} \cos\beta_2 = U - k V_{r1} \cos\beta_1$ (assuming $\beta_2$ makes $V_{r2}$ component oppose U direction when $V_{w2}$ is positive)

$W = U(V_{w1} - V_{w2}) = U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - (U - k V_{r1} \cos\beta_1))$
If $\beta_1 = \beta_2$ and $V_{r1}\cos\beta_1 = V_1\cos\alpha_1 - U$:
$W = U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - (U - k(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)))$
$W = U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U + k V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - kU)$
$W = U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 (1+k) - U(1+k)) = U(1+k)(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$

Energy supplied to blades = $\frac{1}{2}V_1^2$.

$\eta_b = \frac{U(1+k)(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{\frac{1}{2}V_1^2} = \frac{2U(1+k)(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2}$

If frictionless ($k=1$): $W = 2U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$ and $\eta_b = \frac{4U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2}$.

Optimum Blade Speed Ratio ($\rho = U/V_1$) for Maximum Efficiency (frictionless, symmetrical):

$\rho_{opt} = \frac{U}{V_1} = \frac{\cos\alpha_1}{2}$

Maximum blade efficiency:

$\eta_{b,max} = \cos^2\alpha_1$

Reaction Turbine Velocity Diagrams & Performance

In a reaction turbine stage, steam expands in both fixed and moving blades. The moving blades are aerofoil shaped. A common type is the 50% reaction (Parsons) turbine where enthalpy drop is equally shared and velocity diagrams are symmetrical ($\alpha_1 = \beta_2$, $\alpha_2 = \beta_1$, $V_1=V_{r2}$, $V_2=V_{r1}$).

Reaction Turbine Velocity Diagrams & Performance

In a reaction turbine stage, steam expands in both fixed and moving blades. The moving blades are aerofoil shaped. A common type is the 50% reaction (Parsons) turbine where enthalpy drop is equally shared and velocity diagrams are symmetrical ($\alpha_1 = \beta_2$, $\alpha_2 = \beta_1$, $V_1=V_{r2}$, $V_2=V_{r1}$).

Work Done & Blade Efficiency for 50% Reaction Turbine:

Assumptions: $\alpha_1 = \beta_2$, $\beta_1 = \alpha_2$, $V_1=V_{r2}$, $V_2=V_{r1}$.

$V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1$

$V_{w2} = V_2 \cos\alpha_2 = V_{r1} \cos\beta_1$. Since $\beta_1 = \alpha_2$, $V_{w2} = V_{r1} \cos\alpha_2$. Usually for reaction turbines, $V_{w2}$ is negative (directed opposite to $U$), so $W = U(V_{w1} + |V_{w2}|)$.
From inlet triangle: $V_{r1} \cos\beta_1 = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U$.
So, $|V_{w2}| = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U$. (Using symmetry $V_2=V_{r1}$ and $\alpha_2=\beta_1$)

$W = U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 + (V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)) = U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$

Energy supplied to the stage per unit mass (enthalpy drop in the stage): $\Delta h_{stage} = \Delta h_{fixed} + \Delta h_{moving}$. For 50% reaction, these are equal.
$\Delta h_{stage} = (V_1^2 - V_2^2)/2 + (V_{r2}^2 - V_{r1}^2)/2$.
Using symmetry $V_1=V_{r2}$ and $V_2=V_{r1}$: $\Delta h_{stage} = V_1^2 - V_{r1}^2$.
From inlet triangle $V_{r1}^2 = V_1^2 + U^2 - 2UV_1\cos\alpha_1$.
So, $\Delta h_{stage} = V_1^2 - (V_1^2 + U^2 - 2UV_1\cos\alpha_1) = 2UV_1\cos\alpha_1 - U^2$.

$\eta_b = \frac{W}{\Delta h_{stage}} = \frac{U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{2UV_1\cos\alpha_1 - U^2} = \frac{2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U}{2V_1\cos\alpha_1 - U}$ (This simplifies further if $U$ is not zero)
This specific efficiency definition simplifies to 1 if ideal. A more common definition uses KE associated with $V_1$ and change in $V_r$: $\eta_b = \frac{U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2 - U^2 + 2UV_1 \cos\alpha_1}$ (from some texts, depends on definition of energy input for reaction blade)

Optimum Blade Speed Ratio ($\rho = U/V_1$) for Maximum Efficiency (50% reaction, frictionless, symmetrical):

$\rho_{opt} = \frac{U}{V_1} = \cos\alpha_1$

Maximum blade efficiency:

$\eta_{b,max} = \frac{2\cos^2\alpha_1}{1+\cos^2\alpha_1}$ (This is a common result)
When $U/V_1 = \cos\alpha_1$, the work done $W = V_1\cos\alpha_1(2V_1\cos\alpha_1 - V_1\cos\alpha_1) = V_1^2\cos^2\alpha_1$. And $\Delta h_{stage} = 2(V_1\cos\alpha_1)^2 - (V_1\cos\alpha_1)^2 = V_1^2\cos^2\alpha_1$. This suggests $\eta_b=1$ under these ideal conditions if $\Delta h_{stage}$ is used as input. If the denominator is taken as total KE input and generated in moving blade, the $\frac{2\cos^2\alpha_1}{1+\cos^2\alpha_1}$ form arises. Typically, $\eta_{b,max}$ approaches $2\cos^2\alpha_1 / (1+\cos^2\alpha_1)$ or for axial discharge $\alpha_2=90^\circ$, it is $\cos^2\alpha_1$.

Numerical 1: Impulse Turbine Power Calculation

Problem: Steam enters the turbine at 300 m/s when the nozzle angle is 20°. The diameter of turbine is 100cm and it rotates at 3000 rev/min. The blades are equiangular. If the friction loss is 35% of kinetic energy corresponding to relative velocity at inlet, find the power developed by the turbine. The axial thrust is 150 N. (Assume mass flow rate $\dot{m}$ is needed or power per unit mass flow).

Given Data:
  • $V_1 = 300 \text{ m/s}$
  • $\alpha_1 = 20^\circ$
  • $D = 100 \text{ cm} = 1 \text{ m}$
  • $N = 3000 \text{ rev/min}$
  • Equiangular blades: $\beta_1 = \beta_2$
  • Friction loss = 35% of KE of $V_{r1}$ ⇒ $\frac{1}{2}V_{r2}^2 = (1-0.35) \frac{1}{2}V_{r1}^2 = 0.65 \frac{1}{2}V_{r1}^2$
    So, $V_{r2}^2 = 0.65 V_{r1}^2 \implies V_{r2} = \sqrt{0.65} V_{r1} \approx 0.806 V_{r1}$. So, blade velocity coefficient $k \approx 0.806$.
  • Axial Thrust $F_a = 150 \text{ N}$ (This info seems extra if power is asked per unit mass, or $\dot{m}$ is missing).
Solution Steps:
  1. Calculate Blade Speed (U):
    $U = \frac{\pi D N}{60} = \frac{\pi \times 1 \times 3000}{60} = 50\pi \approx 157.08 \text{ m/s}$
  2. Inlet Velocity Triangle:
    $V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 = 300 \cos 20^\circ \approx 300 \times 0.9397 \approx 281.91 \text{ m/s}$
    $V_{f1} = V_1 \sin\alpha_1 = 300 \sin 20^\circ \approx 300 \times 0.3420 \approx 102.61 \text{ m/s}$
    $V_{r1}^2 = V_{f1}^2 + (V_{w1}-U)^2 = (102.61)^2 + (281.91 - 157.08)^2 = 10528.93 + (124.83)^2 = 10528.93 + 15582.53 = 26111.46$
    $V_{r1} = \sqrt{26111.46} \approx 161.59 \text{ m/s}$
    $\tan\beta_1 = \frac{V_{f1}}{V_{w1}-U} = \frac{102.61}{124.83} \approx 0.822 \implies \beta_1 \approx 39.42^\circ$
  3. Outlet Velocity Triangle:

    Equiangular blades: $\beta_2 = \beta_1 \approx 39.42^\circ$.

    $V_{r2} = k \cdot V_{r1} = 0.806 \times 161.59 \approx 130.24 \text{ m/s}$
    $V_{w2} = U - V_{r2} \cos\beta_2 = 157.08 - 130.24 \cos 39.42^\circ = 157.08 - 130.24 \times 0.7725 = 157.08 - 100.61 \approx 56.47 \text{ m/s}$
    (Assuming $V_{w2}$ is in the direction of $U$. If $\beta_2$ definition makes $V_{r2}\cos\beta_2$ negative relative to $V_{w1}-U$, then $V_{w2} = U - V_{r2x}$ where $V_{r2x}$ is the component of $V_{r2}$ along U's line of action).
    A common convention for equiangular impulse blades: $V_{w1} = V_1\cos\alpha_1$, $V_{w2} = (k(V_1\cos\alpha_1 - U) - U)\cos\phi$ where $\phi$ is angle of $V_{w2}$ relative to $U$.
    More directly, $V_{w_2} = V_{r2}\cos\beta_2 - U$ if $\beta_2$ is defined such that $V_{r2}\cos\beta_2 > U$. Or $V_{w_2} = U - V_{r2}\cos\beta_2$ if $V_{r2}\cos\beta_2 < U$ and its projection is in direction of U.
    Let's use $\Delta V_w = (V_{w1}-U) - (- (V_{w2,rel})) = V_{r1}\cos\beta_1 - (-kV_{r1}\cos\beta_2) = V_{r1}\cos\beta_1 (1+k)$ for equiangular.
    $\Delta V_w = V_{r1}\cos\beta_1 (1+k) = (V_1\cos\alpha_1 - U)(1+k) = (281.91 - 157.08)(1+0.806) = 124.83 \times 1.806 \approx 225.44 \text{ m/s}$.
    Work done $W = U \cdot \Delta V_w = 157.08 \times 225.44 \approx 35410.6 \text{ J/kg} = 35.41 \text{ kJ/kg}$

    The axial thrust $F_a = \dot{m}(V_{f1} - V_{f2}) = 150N$. This implies $\dot{m}$ can be found if $V_{f2}$ is calculated. $V_{f2} = V_{r2}\sin\beta_2 = 130.24 \sin 39.42^\circ \approx 130.24 \times 0.635 \approx 82.70 \text{ m/s}$. $\dot{m} (102.61 - 82.70) = 150 \implies \dot{m} \times 19.91 = 150 \implies \dot{m} \approx 150 / 19.91 \approx 7.53 \text{ kg/s}$.

  4. Power Developed:
    $P = \dot{m} W = 7.53 \text{ kg/s} \times 35.41 \text{ kJ/kg} \approx 266.64 \text{ kW}$

Power Developed $\approx 266.64 \text{ kW}$.

Numerical 2: Impulse Turbine Analysis

Problem: In an impulse turbine (single row wheel) the mean diameter of blades is 1.05 m and speed is 3000 r.p.m. Nozzle angle is 18°, ratio of blade speed to steam speed ($U/V_1$) is 0.42, ratio of relative velocity at outlet to inlet ($V_{r2}/V_{r1}$) is 0.84. Outlet angle ($\beta_2$) is 3° less than inlet angle ($\beta_1$). Steam flow $\dot{m} = 10 \text{ kg/s}$. Find: (i) Tangential Thrust, (ii) Axial Thrust, (iii) Resultant Thrust, (iv) Power developed, (v) Blading efficiency.

Given Data:
  • $D = 1.05 \text{ m}$, $N = 3000 \text{ rpm}$
  • $\alpha_1 = 18^\circ$
  • $\rho = U/V_1 = 0.42$
  • $k = V_{r2}/V_{r1} = 0.84$
  • $\beta_2 = \beta_1 - 3^\circ$
  • $\dot{m} = 10 \text{ kg/s}$
Solution Steps:
  1. $U = \frac{\pi D N}{60} = \frac{\pi \times 1.05 \times 3000}{60} \approx 164.93 \text{ m/s}$.
  2. $V_1 = U / 0.42 = 164.93 / 0.42 \approx 392.69 \text{ m/s}$.
  3. Inlet Triangle:
    $V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 = 392.69 \cos 18^\circ \approx 373.45 \text{ m/s}$.
    $V_{f1} = V_1 \sin\alpha_1 = 392.69 \sin 18^\circ \approx 121.34 \text{ m/s}$.
    $\tan\beta_1 = \frac{V_{f1}}{V_{w1}-U} = \frac{121.34}{373.45-164.93} = \frac{121.34}{208.52} \approx 0.5819 \implies \beta_1 \approx 30.19^\circ$.
    $V_{r1} = \frac{V_{f1}}{\sin\beta_1} = \frac{121.34}{\sin 30.19^\circ} \approx \frac{121.34}{0.5029} \approx 241.30 \text{ m/s}$.
  4. Outlet Triangle:
    $\beta_2 = \beta_1 - 3^\circ = 30.19^\circ - 3^\circ = 27.19^\circ$.
    $V_{r2} = k V_{r1} = 0.84 \times 241.30 \approx 202.69 \text{ m/s}$.
    $V_{w2} = U - V_{r2} \cos\beta_2 = 164.93 - 202.69 \cos 27.19^\circ = 164.93 - 202.69 \times 0.890 \approx 164.93 - 180.39 \approx -15.46 \text{ m/s}$. (Negative means $V_{w2}$ is opposite to $U$).
    $V_{f2} = V_{r2} \sin\beta_2 = 202.69 \sin 27.19^\circ \approx 202.69 \times 0.457 \approx 92.63 \text{ m/s}$.
  5. Calculations:
    (i) Tangential Thrust $F_t = \dot{m} (V_{w1} - V_{w2}) = 10 \times (373.45 - (-15.46)) = 10 \times (373.45 + 15.46) = 10 \times 388.91 \approx 3889.1 \text{ N}$.
    (ii) Axial Thrust $F_a = \dot{m} (V_{f1} - V_{f2}) = 10 \times (121.34 - 92.63) = 10 \times 28.71 \approx 287.1 \text{ N}$.
    (iii) Resultant Thrust $F_R = \sqrt{F_t^2 + F_a^2} = \sqrt{(3889.1)^2 + (287.1)^2} \approx \sqrt{15125098 + 82426} \approx \sqrt{15207524} \approx 3899.68 \text{ N}$.
    (iv) Power Developed $P = F_t \times U = 3889.1 \times 164.93 \approx 641460 \text{ W} = 641.46 \text{ kW}$. (Also $P = \dot{m} U (V_{w1}-V_{w2}) = 10 \times 164.93 \times 388.91 \approx 641.46 \text{ kW}$).
    (v) Blading Efficiency $\eta_b = \frac{2 U (V_{w1}-V_{w2})}{V_1^2} = \frac{2 \times 164.93 \times 388.91}{(392.69)^2} = \frac{128292}{154205} \approx 0.8319 \text{ or } 83.19\%$.

(i) Tangential Thrust $\approx 3889.1 \text{ N}$
(ii) Axial Thrust $\approx 287.1 \text{ N}$
(iii) Resultant Thrust $\approx 3899.68 \text{ N}$
(iv) Power Developed $\approx 641.46 \text{ kW}$
(v) Blading Efficiency $\approx 83.19\%$

Numerical 3: Impulse Turbine Blade Angles & Power

Problem: In a single stage impulse turbine, isentropic enthalpy drop $\Delta h_s = 200 \text{ kJ/kg}$ occurs in nozzle (eff. $\eta_N=96\%$, angle $\alpha_1=15^\circ$). Blade velocity coeff. $k_b=0.96$. Ratio of blade speed to steam velocity $U/V_1=0.5$. Steam mass flow $\dot{m}=20\text{kg/s}$. Velocity of steam entering nozzle $V_0=50 \text{ m/s}$. Find: a) Blade angles $\beta_1, \beta_2$ if steam enters smoothly & leaves axially. b) Blade efficiency $\eta_b$. c) Power developed P. d) Axial thrust $F_a$.

Given Data:
  • $\Delta h_s = 200 \text{ kJ/kg} = 200000 \text{ J/kg}$
  • $\eta_N = 0.96$
  • $\alpha_1 = 15^\circ$
  • $k_b = 0.96$
  • $U/V_1 = 0.5 \implies U = 0.5 V_1$
  • $\dot{m} = 20 \text{ kg/s}$
  • $V_0 = 50 \text{ m/s}$ (inlet to nozzle)
Solution Steps:
  1. Actual enthalpy drop in nozzle: $\Delta h_{actual,N} = \eta_N \Delta h_s = 0.96 \times 200 = 192 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  2. Velocity $V_1$ from nozzle: $\Delta h_{actual,N} = \frac{V_1^2 - V_0^2}{2000}$.
    $192 = \frac{V_1^2 - (50)^2}{2000} \implies 192 \times 2000 = V_1^2 - 2500 \implies 384000 = V_1^2 - 2500$.
    $V_1^2 = 384000 + 2500 = 386500 \implies V_1 = \sqrt{386500} \approx 621.69 \text{ m/s}$.
  3. Blade speed $U = 0.5 V_1 = 0.5 \times 621.69 \approx 310.85 \text{ m/s}$.
  4. Inlet Velocity Triangle (Steam enters smoothly $\implies$ blade inlet angle = relative steam angle):
    $V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 = 621.69 \cos 15^\circ \approx 600.35 \text{ m/s}$.
    $V_{f1} = V_1 \sin\alpha_1 = 621.69 \sin 15^\circ \approx 160.90 \text{ m/s}$.
    $\tan\beta_1 = \frac{V_{f1}}{V_{w1}-U} = \frac{160.90}{600.35-310.85} = \frac{160.90}{289.50} \approx 0.5558 \implies \beta_1 \approx 29.06^\circ$.
    $V_{r1} = \frac{V_{f1}}{\sin\beta_1} = \frac{160.90}{\sin 29.06^\circ} \approx \frac{160.90}{0.4857} \approx 331.27 \text{ m/s}$.
  5. Outlet Velocity Triangle (Steam leaves axially $\implies \alpha_2 = 90^\circ \implies V_{w2} = 0$):
    $V_{r2} = k_b V_{r1} = 0.96 \times 331.27 \approx 318.02 \text{ m/s}$.
    Since $V_{w2} = 0$, from outlet triangle: $U = V_{r2} \cos\beta_2$.
    $\cos\beta_2 = U/V_{r2} = 310.85 / 318.02 \approx 0.9775 \implies \beta_2 \approx 12.18^\circ$.
    $V_{f2} = V_2 = V_{r2} \sin\beta_2 = 318.02 \sin 12.18^\circ \approx 318.02 \times 0.211 \approx 67.10 \text{ m/s}$.
  6. Calculations:
    a) Blade angles: $\beta_1 \approx 29.06^\circ$, $\beta_2 \approx 12.18^\circ$.
    b) Blade Efficiency $\eta_b = \frac{2 U (V_{w1}-V_{w2})}{V_1^2} = \frac{2 \times 310.85 \times (600.35 - 0)}{(621.69)^2} = \frac{373204.5}{386500} \approx 0.9656 \text{ or } 96.56\%$. (Note: $U/V_1 = 0.5$. For $V_{w2}=0$, $\eta_{b,max}$ occurs if $U/V_1 = \cos\alpha_1/2 = \cos 15^\circ / 2 \approx 0.9659/2 \approx 0.483$. Since $0.5$ is close to optimal, efficiency is high).
    c) Power Developed $P = \dot{m} U (V_{w1}-V_{w2}) = 20 \times 310.85 \times (600.35 - 0) \approx 3732045 \text{ W} = 3732.05 \text{ kW}$.
    d) Axial Thrust $F_a = \dot{m} (V_{f1}-V_{f2}) = 20 \times (160.90 - 67.10) = 20 \times 93.80 = 1876 \text{ N}$.

a) $\beta_1 \approx 29.06^\circ$, $\beta_2 \approx 12.18^\circ$
b) Blade Efficiency $\eta_b \approx 96.56\%$
c) Power Developed $P \approx 3732.05 \text{ kW}$
d) Axial Thrust $F_a \approx 1876 \text{ N}$

Numerical 4: Reaction Turbine Analysis

Problem: A reaction turbine uses 9000 kg/hr of steam. Blades are 20 mm high. Discharge angle of both fixed and moving blades is 20° (i.e., $\alpha_1 = \beta_2 = 20^\circ$). Steam leaves fixed blades at 0.32 N/mm² (3.2 bar) with dryness $x=0.95$ and velocity $V_1 = 120 \text{ m/s}$. Ratio of axial velocity to blade velocity $V_f/U = 0.7$ at entry and $0.76$ at exit from moving blades. Tip leakage = 6%. Determine: i) Speed of turbine (RPM), ii) Power developed.

Given Data:
  • $\dot{m}_{total} = 9000 \text{ kg/hr} = 9000/3600 = 2.5 \text{ kg/s}$
  • Tip leakage = 6% $\implies$ Actual mass flow over blades $\dot{m} = 2.5 \times (1-0.06) = 2.5 \times 0.94 = 2.35 \text{ kg/s}$.
  • Blade height $h_b = 20 \text{ mm} = 0.02 \text{ m}$.
  • Fixed blade exit angle $\alpha_1 = 20^\circ$. Moving blade exit angle $\beta_2 = 20^\circ$. (Suggests symmetrical blading for 50% reaction).
  • Steam leaves fixed blades at $P_1=3.2 \text{ bar}$, $x_1=0.95$, $V_1=120 \text{ m/s}$.
  • At moving blade entry: $V_{f1}/U = 0.7$.
  • At moving blade exit: $V_{f2}/U = 0.76$.
Solution Steps:
  1. From $V_1$ and $\alpha_1$:
    $V_{f1} = V_1 \sin\alpha_1 = 120 \sin 20^\circ \approx 120 \times 0.3420 \approx 41.04 \text{ m/s}$.
    $V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1 = 120 \cos 20^\circ \approx 120 \times 0.9397 \approx 112.76 \text{ m/s}$.
  2. Blade speed $U$:
    $V_{f1}/U = 0.7 \implies U = V_{f1}/0.7 = 41.04 / 0.7 \approx 58.63 \text{ m/s}$.
  3. For 50% reaction turbine with symmetrical blading ($\alpha_1=\beta_2$, $\beta_1=\alpha_2$, $V_1=V_{r2}$, $V_2=V_{r1}$):
    Work done $W = U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$ per kg.
    $W = 58.63 \times (2 \times 120 \cos 20^\circ - 58.63) = 58.63 \times (2 \times 112.76 - 58.63)$
    $W = 58.63 \times (225.52 - 58.63) = 58.63 \times 166.89 \approx 9784.8 \text{ J/kg} = 9.7848 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  4. ii) Power Developed $P = \dot{m} W = 2.35 \text{ kg/s} \times 9.7848 \text{ kJ/kg} \approx 22.99 \text{ kW}$.
  5. To find RPM, we need mean blade diameter $D_m$.
    Mass flow rate through blades: $\dot{m} = \rho_1 (\pi D_m h_b) V_{f1}$.
    We need $\rho_1$. At $P_1=3.2 \text{ bar}$, $x_1=0.95$:
    From steam tables at 3.2 bar: $v_f \approx 0.001076 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg}$, $v_g \approx 0.5596 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg}$.
    $v_1 = v_f + x_1(v_g - v_f) = 0.001076 + 0.95(0.5596 - 0.001076) \approx 0.001076 + 0.95 \times 0.558524 \approx 0.001076 + 0.53059 \approx 0.53167 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg}$.
    $\rho_1 = 1/v_1 \approx 1/0.53167 \approx 1.8808 \text{ kg/m}^3$.
    $2.35 = 1.8808 \times (\pi D_m \times 0.02) \times 41.04$.
    $2.35 = D_m \times (1.8808 \times \pi \times 0.02 \times 41.04) \approx D_m \times 4.838$.
    $D_m = 2.35 / 4.838 \approx 0.4857 \text{ m}$.
  6. i) Speed of turbine $N$: $U = \frac{\pi D_m N}{60} \implies N = \frac{60 U}{\pi D_m} = \frac{60 \times 58.63}{\pi \times 0.4857} \approx \frac{3517.8}{1.5259} \approx 2305.4 \text{ rpm}$.
    Check $V_{f2}/U = 0.76 \implies V_{f2} = 0.76 \times U = 0.76 \times 58.63 \approx 44.56 \text{ m/s}$. This information can be used to find $\beta_1, \alpha_2$ if needed, but not directly for power or RPM with the 50% reaction work formula.

i) Speed of turbine $\approx 2305.4 \text{ rpm}$
ii) Power developed $\approx 22.99 \text{ kW}$

Note: Calculations for reaction turbines can be complex. The assumption of 50% reaction symmetrical blading simplifies work done. If $V_{f2}/U$ was critical, it would imply solving full outlet triangles.

MCQ:

1. In a velocity diagram for a turbine blade, $V_{r1}$ represents:

  • a) Absolute velocity of steam at inlet to the nozzle.
  • b) Absolute velocity of steam at inlet to the moving blade.
  • c) Relative velocity of steam at inlet to the moving blade.
  • d) Blade velocity.

Answer: c) Relative velocity of steam at inlet to the moving blade.

2. The work done by steam on the blades in an axial flow turbine stage is primarily due to the change in:

  • a) Axial velocity of steam ($V_f$).
  • b) Tangential velocity (whirl velocity) of steam ($V_w$).
  • c) Blade velocity ($U$).
  • d) Static pressure of steam across the blades.

Answer: b) Tangential velocity (whirl velocity) of steam ($V_w$).

3. For a single-stage impulse turbine with symmetrical blades and no friction, maximum blade efficiency ($\cos^2 \alpha_1$) occurs when the blade speed ratio ($U/V_1$) is:

  • a) $\cos \alpha_1$
  • b) $\sin \alpha_1$
  • c) $0.5 \cos \alpha_1$
  • d) $2 \cos \alpha_1$

Answer: c) $0.5 \cos \alpha_1$

4. If the absolute velocity of steam leaving the moving blades ($V_2$) is purely axial, then:

  • a) The whirl component $V_{w2}$ is maximum.
  • b) The whirl component $V_{w2}$ is zero.
  • c) The blade efficiency is zero.
  • d) The blade speed $U$ is zero.

Answer: b) The whirl component $V_{w2}$ is zero. (This condition leads to maximum work extraction for a given $V_{w1}$ and $U$ for impulse turbines, and is a design goal for maximum efficiency in many cases.)

5. The blade velocity coefficient 'k' ($V_{r2} = k \cdot V_{r1}$) accounts for:

  • a) Pressure drop in the nozzles.
  • b) Friction losses as steam flows over the moving blades.
  • c) Heat loss from the turbine casing.
  • d) Change in blade velocity $U$.

Answer: b) Friction losses as steam flows over the moving blades.

6. For an impulse turbine stage, the "energy supplied to the blades" in the blade efficiency formula is typically:

  • a) Enthalpy drop in the nozzles.
  • b) Kinetic energy of steam leaving the nozzles ($V_1^2/2$).
  • c) Kinetic energy of steam leaving the moving blades ($V_2^2/2$).
  • d) Total enthalpy drop in the stage.

Answer: b) Kinetic energy of steam leaving the nozzles ($V_1^2/2$).
