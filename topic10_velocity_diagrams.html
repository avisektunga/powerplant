Decoding Steam's Journey: Introduction to Velocity Diagrams

Imagine you're a sports commentator analyzing a fast-paced game. You'd want to know the speed and direction of every player, right? **Velocity diagrams (or velocity triangles)** are the engineer's "instant replay" for steam moving through a turbine. They are super important drawings (vector diagrams) that show us exactly how fast the steam is going and in what direction as it interacts with the stationary nozzles and the spinning moving blades.

Why bother? Because these diagrams help us figure out:

  • How much energy the steam gives to the blades.
  • The forces acting on the blades (so they don't break!).
  • How much work the turbine stage is doing.
  • How efficient the blades are at their job.

Self-Question: If steam is invisible, how do we even know these velocities?

Answer: Great question! Engineers use thermodynamic principles (like the energy equation) and fluid dynamics to calculate these velocities based on pressures, temperatures, and the shapes of nozzles and blades. The diagrams then visualize these calculated values.

Meet the Players in Our Velocity "Game" (Key Components):

  • $U$: **Blade Velocity.** How fast the part of the blade we're looking at is moving in a circle. Think of the speed of a horse on a merry-go-round. ($U = \pi D N / 60$).
  • $V_1$: **Absolute Steam Velocity at Inlet.** How fast steam is actually moving (and in what direction) just as it's about to hit the moving blade. It just came out of a nozzle, so it's usually pretty fast!
  • $\alpha_1$: **Nozzle Angle.** The angle at which the steam jet ($V_1$) is fired at the moving blades.
  • $V_{r1}$: **Relative Steam Velocity at Inlet.** Imagine you're *riding on the moving blade*. This is how fast the steam *appears* to be coming towards you. It's the vector difference: $\vec{V_{r1}} = \vec{V_1} - \vec{U}$.
  • $\beta_1$: **Blade Inlet Angle.** The angle of the leading edge of the moving blade. Ideally, this matches the angle of $V_{r1}$ so the steam slides on smoothly without "splashing" (shock losses).
  • $V_{r2}$: **Relative Steam Velocity at Outlet.** How fast the steam *appears* to be leaving, to someone still on the moving blade.
  • $\beta_2$: **Blade Outlet Angle.** The angle of the trailing edge of the moving blade.
  • $V_2$: **Absolute Steam Velocity at Outlet.** How fast steam is actually moving (and in what direction) after it has passed over the moving blade and is heading to the next part of the turbine (or the exhaust). $\vec{V_2} = \vec{V_{r2}} + \vec{U}$.
  • $\alpha_2$: **Absolute Steam Exit Angle.** The angle of $V_2$.
  • $V_{w1}, V_{w2}$: **Whirl Velocities.** These are the "sideways push" components of $V_1$ and $V_2$ (in the direction of blade motion $U$). They are the superstars for doing work! $V_{w1} = V_1 \cos\alpha_1$, $V_{w2} = V_2 \cos\alpha_2$.
  • $V_{f1}, V_{f2}$: **Flow Velocities.** These are the "straight-through" components of $V_1$ and $V_2$ (axial direction). They push steam through the turbine and cause axial thrust. $V_{f1} = V_1 \sin\alpha_1$, $V_{f2} = V_2 \sin\alpha_2$.
Analogy: Throwing a Ball from a Moving Train
If you throw a ball straight out from a moving train ($V_{r1}$), someone standing on the ground ($V_1$) sees the ball moving forward with the train's speed ($U$) *plus* the speed you threw it at. Velocity triangles help sort out these combined motions!

The Golden Rule for Work (Euler's Turbine Equation):
Work Done per kg of steam = (Blade Speed $U$) $\times$ (Change in Whirl Velocity $\Delta V_w$).
Power Developed = (Mass flow rate of steam $\dot{m}$) $\times$ (Work Done per kg).
And Blade Efficiency is basically: (Work the blades got) / (Energy the steam jet had).

Impulse Turbines: The "Hit and Glide" Steam Dance

Remember impulse turbines? Steam gets its speed boost in the nozzles *before* hitting the blades. Ideally, pressure doesn't change as steam glides over the moving blades. The blades are often symmetrical (bucket-shaped, so $\beta_1 \approx \beta_2$).

Self-Question: In the diagram, why is $V_{r2}$ often shown slightly shorter than $V_{r1}$ even if blades are symmetrical?

Answer: Friction! As steam rubs against the blade surface, it loses a tiny bit of its relative speed. We use a "blade velocity coefficient" ($k = V_{r2}/V_{r1}$, where $k < 1$) to account for this. If no friction, $k=1$ and $V_{r2}=V_{r1}$.

Work & Efficiency Secrets of Impulse Blades:

For symmetrical blades ($\beta_1 = \beta_2$) and blade friction coefficient $k$:

Work Done, $W = U(1+k)(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$

Blade Efficiency (Energy In = $\frac{1}{2}V_1^2$):

$\eta_b = \frac{2U(1+k)(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2}$

If totally frictionless ($k=1$): $W = 2U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$ and $\eta_b = \frac{4U(V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{V_1^2}$.

The Sweet Spot for Speed (Optimum Blade Speed Ratio $\rho = U/V_1$ for Max Efficiency):
For that frictionless, symmetrical impulse blade, the best blade speed is:

$\rho_{opt} = \frac{U}{V_1} = \frac{\cos\alpha_1}{2}$

And at this perfect speed, the maximum possible blade efficiency is:

$\eta_{b,max} = \cos^2\alpha_1$

This usually happens when the steam leaves the blades going straight axially ($V_{w2}=0$), meaning no whirl energy is wasted!

Reaction Turbines: The "Expand and Push" Steam Dance

Reaction turbines are different. Steam expands (pressure drops, speed increases) in *both* the fixed guide blades *and* the moving blades. The moving blades are shaped like little airplane wings (aerofoils) or nozzles. A classic is the 50% reaction (Parsons) turbine, where the energy drop is split evenly, and the velocity diagrams look quite symmetrical.

Self-Question: If steam expands in moving blades, does that mean the relative speed $V_{r2}$ (leaving) is faster than $V_{r1}$ (entering)?

Answer: Yes, exactly! That acceleration of steam out of the moving blade gives it an extra "kick" or reaction force, contributing to the turbine's spin.

Work & Efficiency Insights for 50% Reaction Blades:

Assuming ideal symmetrical blading ($\alpha_1 = \beta_2$, $\beta_1 = \alpha_2$, so $V_1=V_{r2}$ and $V_2=V_{r1}$):

Work Done, $W = U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)$

Energy input to the stage for efficiency calculation is the total enthalpy drop in the stage ($\Delta h_{stage}$). For 50% reaction, this is $\Delta h_{stage} = 2UV_1\cos\alpha_1 - U^2$ (under ideal conditions).

Blade Efficiency, $\eta_b = \frac{W}{\Delta h_{stage}} = \frac{U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U)}{2UV_1\cos\alpha_1 - U^2}$
(This ideally simplifies to 100% for frictionless flow if the denominator is correctly defined as the total energy made available in the stage for work production.)

The Sweet Spot for Speed (Optimum Blade Speed Ratio $\rho = U/V_1$ for Max Efficiency):
For these ideal 50% reaction blades, the best blade speed is:

$\rho_{opt} = \frac{U}{V_1} = \cos\alpha_1$

And the maximum blade efficiency (a commonly cited result for these conditions) is:

$\eta_{b,max} = \frac{2\cos^2\alpha_1}{1+\cos^2\alpha_1}$

Notice the optimum speed ratio is different from the impulse turbine! This means reaction turbines often spin "faster" relative to the steam jet speed $V_1$ for best performance, or require more stages for the same overall pressure drop if blade speeds are similar.

Previous Year Questions & Solved Numericals:

2017: Explain how turbine performance is evaluated using blade efficiency.

Blade efficiency (or diagram efficiency) is like a "report card" for how well a specific row of turbine blades converts the steam's energy into useful rotational work on the shaft. It tells us if the blades are good "energy catchers."

The Basic Idea:
Blade Efficiency ($\eta_b$) = $\frac{\text{Actual Work Done by Steam on Blades}}{\text{Energy Supplied to those Blades}}$

How it Helps Evaluate Performance:

  1. Good Design? High blade efficiency means the blade shapes, angles, and the speed at which they are running ($U$) are well-matched to the incoming steam speed ($V_1$) and angle ($\alpha_1$). Engineers use it to optimize designs!
  2. Spotting Problems (Losses): If efficiency is lower than expected, it's a clue that energy is being wasted. Where?
    • Friction: Steam rubbing against blades.
    • "Splash" Losses (Incidence): Steam hitting blades at the wrong angle.
    • Leakage: Steam sneaking around blade tips (especially in reaction turbines).
  3. Comparing Apples to Apples: Allows engineers to compare different blade designs fairly.
  4. Part of the Bigger Picture: Good blade efficiency is essential for good *overall* turbine efficiency (which also includes losses in nozzles, between stages, etc.).

Self-Question: If my blade efficiency is 100%, does that mean my whole turbine is 100% efficient?

Answer: Not quite! Blade efficiency only looks at how well *that specific row of blades* works. There are still losses in the nozzles that prepare the steam jet, kinetic energy lost in the steam leaving the stage ("leaving loss"), mechanical friction in bearings, etc. But super-efficient blades are a big step towards a super-efficient turbine!

So, by calculating and analyzing blade efficiency using velocity diagrams, engineers can understand and improve how well each part of the turbine is "dancing" with the steam to extract its power.

Numerical 1: Impulse Turbine Power Calculation

Problem: Steam enters the turbine at 300 m/s when the nozzle angle is 20°. The diameter of turbine is 100cm and it rotates at 3000 rev/min. The blades are equiangular. If the friction loss is 35% of kinetic energy corresponding to relative velocity at inlet, find the power developed by the turbine. The axial thrust is 150 N.

Given Data: $V_1 = 300 \text{ m/s}$, $\alpha_1 = 20^\circ$, $D = 1 \text{ m}$, $N = 3000 \text{ rpm}$, $\beta_1 = \beta_2$. Friction loss $\implies k \approx 0.806$. $F_a = 150 \text{ N}$.

Solution Outline:

  1. Calculate Blade Speed $U \approx 157.08 \text{ m/s}$.
  2. Solve Inlet Triangle: $V_{w1} \approx 281.91 \text{ m/s}$, $V_{f1} \approx 102.61 \text{ m/s}$, $V_{r1} \approx 161.59 \text{ m/s}$, $\beta_1 \approx 39.42^\circ$.
  3. Solve Outlet Triangle: $\beta_2 = \beta_1$. $V_{r2} = k V_{r1} \approx 130.24 \text{ m/s}$. $\Delta V_w = (V_1\cos\alpha_1 - U)(1+k) \approx 225.44 \text{ m/s}$.
  4. Work Done $W = U \Delta V_w \approx 35.41 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  5. Mass flow $\dot{m}$ from $F_a = \dot{m}(V_{f1}-V_{f2})$. ($V_{f2} = V_{r2}\sin\beta_2 \approx 82.70 \text{ m/s}$). $\dot{m} \approx 7.53 \text{ kg/s}$.
  6. Power $P = \dot{m} W$.

Power Developed $\approx 266.64 \text{ kW}$.

Numerical 2: Impulse Turbine Analysis

Problem: Impulse turbine: $D=1.05\text{m}$, $N=3000\text{rpm}$, $\alpha_1=18^\circ$, $U/V_1=0.42$, $V_{r2}/V_{r1}=0.84$, $\beta_2=\beta_1-3^\circ$, $\dot{m}=10\text{kg/s}$. Find Thrusts, Power, $\eta_b$.

  3. Outlet: $\beta_2 \approx 27.19^\circ$, $V_{r2} \approx 202.69 \text{ m/s}$, $V_{w2} \approx -15.46 \text{ m/s}$, $V_{f2} \approx 92.63 \text{ m/s}$.

(i) Tangential Thrust $F_t \approx 3889.1 \text{ N}$
(ii) Axial Thrust $F_a \approx 287.1 \text{ N}$
(iii) Resultant Thrust $F_R \approx 3899.68 \text{ N}$
(iv) Power Developed $P \approx 641.46 \text{ kW}$
(v) Blading Efficiency $\eta_b \approx 83.19\%$

Numerical 4: Reaction Turbine Analysis

Problem: Reaction turbine: $\dot{m}_{total}=9000\text{kg/hr}$, $h_b=20\text{mm}$, $\alpha_1=\beta_2=20^\circ$. Steam ex-fixed blades: $P_1=3.2\text{bar}, x_1=0.95, V_1=120\text{m/s}$. $V_{f1}/U=0.7$, $V_{f2}/U=0.76$. Tip leakage=6%. Find RPM, Power.

Solution Outline:

  1. $\dot{m}_{actual} = 2.35 \text{ kg/s}$.
  2. $V_{f1} \approx 41.04 \text{ m/s}$. $U = V_{f1}/0.7 \approx 58.63 \text{ m/s}$.
  3. Work (assuming 50% reaction symmetrical): $W = U(2V_1 \cos\alpha_1 - U) \approx 9.7848 \text{ kJ/kg}$.
  4. Power $P = \dot{m} W \approx 22.99 \text{ kW}$.
  5. Specific volume $v_1 \approx 0.53167 \text{ m}^3/\text{kg}$ ($\rho_1 \approx 1.8808 \text{ kg/m}^3$).
  6. Mean Diameter $D_m$ from $\dot{m} = \rho_1 (\pi D_m h_b) V_{f1} \implies D_m \approx 0.4857 \text{ m}$.
  7. Speed $N = \frac{60 U}{\pi D_m}$.

i) Speed of turbine $N \approx 2305.4 \text{ rpm}$
ii) Power developed $P \approx 22.99 \text{ kW}$

MCQ Speed Challenge: Test Your Triangle Smarts!

1. If you're on a moving turbine blade and steam seems to hit you from a certain angle, that's its:

  • a) Absolute velocity ($V_1$)
  • b) Blade velocity ($U$)
  • c) Relative velocity ($V_{r1}$)
  • d) Whirl velocity ($V_{w1}$)

Answer: c) Relative velocity ($V_{r1}$)

2. The "whirl" component of steam velocity ($V_w$) is the part that directly:

  • a) Pushes steam axially through the turbine.
  • b) Creates the tangential force to spin the rotor.
  • c) Measures the steam's temperature.
  • d) Determines the steam's dryness fraction.

Answer: b) Creates the tangential force to spin the rotor.

3. For a perfect single-stage impulse turbine with nozzle angle $\alpha_1=0^\circ$ (steam fired straight), what's the optimum blade speed $U$ for max efficiency if $V_1=400$ m/s?

  • a) 400 m/s
  • b) 200 m/s
  • c) 100 m/s
  • d) 0 m/s

Answer: b) 200 m/s (Since $\rho_{opt} = U/V_1 = \cos\alpha_1/2$. If $\alpha_1=0$, $\cos\alpha_1=1$. So $U = V_1/2 = 400/2 = 200$ m/s).

4. If steam leaves the moving blades of a turbine stage going perfectly straight axially ($\alpha_2 = 90^\circ$), it means:

  • a) The work done in that stage is zero.
  • b) The velocity of whirl at exit ($V_{w2}$) is zero.
  • c) All kinetic energy has been lost.
  • d) The blade speed ($U$) must be very high.

Answer: b) The velocity of whirl at exit ($V_{w2}$) is zero. (This is desirable for maximizing work in impulse stages!)

5. The blade velocity coefficient 'k' is less than 1 ($k<1$) because of:

  • a) Steam expanding in the moving blades.
  • b) The high speed of the rotor.
  • c) Friction between the steam and the blade surfaces.
  • d) Heat transfer from the steam to the blades.

Answer: c) Friction between the steam and the blade surfaces.

6. In the formula for blade efficiency of an impulse turbine, the "Energy Supplied to Blades" is usually the:

  • a) Enthalpy drop across the entire turbine.
  • b) Kinetic energy of steam leaving the nozzles ($V_1^2/2$).
  • c) Work done by the blades.
  • d) Heat added in the boiler.

Answer: b) Kinetic energy of steam leaving the nozzles ($V_1^2/2$).
